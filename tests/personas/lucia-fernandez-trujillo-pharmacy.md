# Persona: Lucía Fernández

## Profile
- **Name:** Lucía Fernández Castillo
- **Age:** 35
- **Location:** Trujillo, Peru (Urbanización El Recreo, zona residencial)
- **Business:** Farmacia Santa Rosa — neighborhood pharmacy serving a residential community of ~5,000 families. Named after the local parish.
- **Products/Services:** Prescription medications, OTC products, personal care, baby products, medical supplies, blood pressure/glucose testing service (S/5 per test)
- **Team size:** 4 employees (1 licensed pharmacist/director técnico — Lucía herself, 2 pharmacy technicians, 1 cashier/stock clerk)
- **Annual revenue:** ~S/720,000 (~$192K USD)
- **Language:** Spanish (native, norteño accent — "churre" for kid, "chamba" for work)
- **Communication style:** Precise, professional when discussing medications, informal with regular customers. Uses abbreviations common in pharma (mg, ml, tab, cap). Texts fast with some typos. Sends photos of medication labels to confirm products.
- **Payment methods:** Cash (50%), Yape (30%), BCP debit/credit POS (15%), insurance claims/vouchers (5%)
- **Tax ID (RUC):** 10345678901
- **Tax regime:** Régimen Especial de Renta (RER) — simplified, 1.5% monthly income tax
- **SUNAT obligations:** Boletas de venta (individual customers), facturas (institutional/insurance), monthly IGV declaration, RER simplified income tax
- **Regulatory:** DIGEMID-licensed pharmacy, controlled substance registry, MINSA health protocols

## Daily Tasks (what Lucía actually does every day)
1. Open pharmacy at 7:30am — review overnight orders and low stock alerts
2. Process 100-130 transactions per day (average ticket S/18-25)
3. Verify prescriptions for controlled substances (tramadol, clonazepam, etc.) — log in SISMED
4. Check expiry dates on front-facing products — rotate stock (FEFO: First Expired, First Out)
5. Receive deliveries from distributors (DROKASA, Química Suiza, ALBIS) — verify quantities against purchase orders
6. Process insurance vouchers (EPS: Rímac, Pacífico, La Positiva) — match to prescriptions
7. Attend walk-in health consultations (blood pressure checks, basic medication advice)
8. Reorder fast-moving products before they run out — amoxicilina, paracetamol, omeprazol always
9. Close cash register — reconcile cash, Yape, and POS terminal
10. Update DIGEMID controlled substance log

## Weekly Tasks
1. Submit insurance claims batch (EPS vouchers for reimbursement)
2. Place restock orders to 3-4 distributors based on sales velocity
3. Full expiry date audit — pull products expiring within 90 days
4. Reconcile bank account (BCP) with POS terminal and Yape settlements
5. Pay supplier invoices (most are net-15 or net-30 terms)
6. Review slow-moving inventory — plan promotions to clear before expiry
7. SUNAT batch boleta/factura processing for the week
8. Update price tags for products with manufacturer price changes
9. Train technicians on new products or protocol updates
10. Clean and calibrate blood pressure and glucose monitoring equipment

## Monthly Tasks
1. SUNAT monthly IGV declaration
2. RER income tax payment (1.5% of net monthly income)
3. PLAME payroll declaration and EsSalud contributions
4. DIGEMID controlled substance monthly report
5. Full physical inventory count (spot-check weekly, full monthly)
6. Reconcile insurance claims — which were paid, which rejected, which pending
7. Calculate profit margins by product category (generics vs. branded vs. OTC vs. personal care)
8. Review and renew supplier agreements and negotiate volume discounts
9. Order seasonal products (sunscreen in summer, flu meds in winter)
10. Evaluate new generics to add to inventory (better margins than branded)
11. Prepare for DIGEMID inspection readiness (can happen any month)

## Pain Points (what keeps Lucía up at night)
- **Expiry date management:** 2,000+ SKUs, each with different expiry dates. Products expire on shelves = pure loss. No automated tracking.
- **Insurance claims hell:** EPS companies reject claims for minor documentation errors. Resubmission is manual and time-consuming. Payment takes 45-90 days.
- **Controlled substance compliance:** DIGEMID requires meticulous logging. One error = pharmacy shutdown risk. Paper-based log is error-prone.
- **Thin margins:** Generics: 25-30% margin. Branded: 15-20%. Personal care: 35-40%. Overall average ~22%. No room for waste.
- **High transaction volume:** 100+ sales/day means can't verify every Yape screenshot manually. Mismatches pile up.
- **Supplier management:** 6+ distributors with different payment terms, pricing, minimums. No unified view.
- **Stock forecasting:** Out-of-stock on critical meds = customers go to competition. Overstock = expiry risk. No demand analytics.
- **Cash-heavy business:** Handling S/2,000+ in cash daily — security risk, reconciliation headache.
- **Price sensitivity:** Customers compare prices with nearby pharmacies and Inkafarma/MiFarma chains. Must stay competitive.
- **No analytics:** Doesn't know which product categories drive profit, which customers are regulars, or seasonal demand patterns.
