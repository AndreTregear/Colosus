# Persona: Marco Silva

## Profile
- **Name:** Marco Silva Zúñiga
- **Age:** 40
- **Location:** Arequipa, Peru (Zona Industrial de Cayma, taller en casa familiar)
- **Business:** Textiles Sillar EIRL — manufactures and sells alpaca wool textiles (scarves, ponchos, blankets, sweaters). Named after Arequipa's iconic white volcanic stone.
- **Products/Services:** Handwoven and semi-industrial alpaca textiles. B2B wholesale to boutiques in Lima/Cusco (60%), direct retail at workshop showroom (25%), international exports to USA/Europe (15%)
- **Team size:** 12 employees (8 weavers, 2 quality control/finishing, 1 warehouse/shipping, 1 admin/sales)
- **Annual revenue:** ~S/850,000 (~$226K USD)
- **Language:** Spanish (native, Arequipeño accent — "ccaccau", "loncco"), basic English (for export correspondence)
- **Communication style:** Methodical, detail-oriented, sends product photos with specifications, uses WhatsApp for B2B orders, prefers written over voice, uses textile jargon naturally
- **Payment methods:** BCP/BBVA bank transfer (B2B domestic — 70%), international wire SWIFT (exports — 15%), Yape (retail walk-ins — 10%), cash (5%)
- **Tax ID (RUC):** 20456789123
- **Tax regime:** Régimen General (due to revenue level and export activity)
- **SUNAT obligations:** Facturas electrónicas (all B2B), boletas (retail), monthly IGV, annual income tax, export documentation (facturas de exportación), drawback claims

## Daily Tasks (what Marco actually does every day)
1. Check production floor — monitor 8 looms and weaver progress against orders
2. Respond to WhatsApp inquiries from boutique clients in Lima/Cusco
3. Review raw material inventory (alpaca fiber by grade: baby alpaca, superfine, huacaya)
4. Process 2-5 B2B orders and confirm delivery dates
5. Coordinate with courier services (Olva Courier, Shalom) for domestic shipments
6. Quality check finished pieces before packaging
7. Update production tracking — which orders are on the loom, which are finishing
8. Photograph new designs for catalog and WhatsApp broadcast
9. Respond to export inquiries (email, sometimes in English)
10. Pay daily labor for piece-rate weavers

## Weekly Tasks
1. Place raw material orders to alpaca fiber suppliers in Puno/Junín
2. Review production schedule and prioritize based on delivery deadlines
3. Ship completed B2B orders to Lima/Cusco boutiques
4. Process weekly payroll for piece-rate weavers (by meters woven)
5. Reconcile bank transfers received vs. invoices issued
6. Update product catalog with new designs and colorways
7. Follow up on outstanding payments from boutique clients (net-30 terms)
8. Batch emit facturas electrónicas for the week's B2B sales
9. Coordinate with dye supplier for custom color batches
10. Review shipping status of outbound orders

## Monthly Tasks
1. SUNAT monthly IGV declaration (18% — but exports are zero-rated)
2. Income tax advance payment (pagos a cuenta — 1.5% of gross under Régimen General)
3. PLAME payroll declaration and EsSalud contributions for formal employees
4. Calculate production costs per product line (fiber + labor + overhead)
5. Negotiate pricing with alpaca fiber cooperatives for next month
6. Process drawback claims for exports (SUNAT refund of 3% FOB value)
7. Review inventory levels and plan production for next month
8. Prepare export documentation: Certificate of Origin, packing list, commercial invoice
9. Update B2B price list based on fiber cost fluctuations
10. Meet with freight forwarder about upcoming international shipments
11. Evaluate new designs based on sales data and boutique feedback
12. Review cash flow — manage gap between production costs and B2B payment terms

## Pain Points (what keeps Marco up at night)
- **Production scheduling chaos:** 12 active orders across 8 looms, each with different fiber, pattern, and deadline. Tracks everything on a whiteboard.
- **Raw material price volatility:** Baby alpaca fiber can swing 20% between seasons. Locks in prices too early = overpays. Too late = no stock.
- **B2B payment delays:** Boutiques in Lima pay net-30 but often stretch to net-60. Cash flow crunch while paying weavers weekly.
- **Export documentation complexity:** Certificates of origin, phytosanitary certs, commercial invoices, packing lists — one error delays shipment weeks.
- **No production analytics:** Doesn't know true cost per piece, which products are most profitable, or which weavers are most productive.
- **Inventory tracking:** Has 40+ SKUs across fiber grades, colors, and product types. Counts manually. Often oversells.
- **Shipping logistics:** Domestic (Olva/Shalom) and international (DHL/FedEx) with different tracking, pricing, insurance needs.
- **SUNAT drawback claims:** Entitled to 3% FOB refund on exports but the paperwork is complex and time-consuming.
- **Weaver retention:** Skilled weavers are scarce. Needs to keep them busy and well-paid or they leave.
- **Quality inconsistency:** Handmade products vary. International buyers expect exact color matching and dimensions.
