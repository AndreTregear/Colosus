## Executive Summary

Peru's microenterprise economy represents one of Latin America's most paradoxical markets: a nation where 99.6% of all businesses are micro and small enterprises (MYPEs), yet 86.8% of them operate informally. With over 6 million MYPEs generating 14.2% of GDP and employing 45.2% of the economically active population, the gap between economic reality and formal financial access defines the core opportunity for fintech innovation. The total MSE credit market is estimated at **USD $23.7 billion** (CGAP, 2021), yet only 27.8% of MYPEs have access to formal credit. This document maps the full landscape—from INEI demographic data to SBS credit portfolios, microfinance institution dynamics, factoring markets, digital payment adoption, and the role of WhatsApp as informal commercial infrastructure—to inform Yaya Platform's market entry strategy.

- **2024 national labor informality rate:** 70.9% (INEI EPEN)
- Down from 74% in 2022, but still affecting **12.4 million workers** (MTPE registry)
- **5.2 million informal dependent workers**, primarily in MYPEs
- **2.1 million informal emprendimientos** identified
- MTPE has identified **15 types of informality** (8 self-employment, 7 dependent)

**Sources:** INEI EPEN 2024 via Gestión; MTPE/PQS 2025

### 2.5 What Formalization Looks Like

Peru's government goal: reach >30% formal employment before end of term. Key formalization barriers:
- **Cost:** Registration fees, tax compliance burden
- **Complexity:** Multiple regulatory agencies (SUNAT, SUNAFIL, municipal licenses)
- **Lack of incentives:** Informal businesses see no benefit from formalization
- **Credit scoring void:** Informal businesses have no credit history, creating a catch-22
- **ICF Index (2024):** ComexPerú's "Índice de Capacidad Formal" declined 0.8% in 2024

**Source:** Gestión Feb 2025; ComexPerú July 2025

## 3. The SMB Credit Gap

### 3.1 Total Market Size

CGAP's 2021 market sizing exercise estimated Peru's MSE credit market at approximately **USD $23.7 billion**. This includes formal and informal lending sources.

| Credit Source | Share of Total | Annual Volume (est.) |
|--------------|----------------|---------------------|
| Banks and MFIs (formal) | ~25% | ~USD $12.3 bn |
| Family and informal lenders | Largest informal source | ~USD $3.9 bn |
| Fintechs | **0.3%** | Minimal |
| Other (cooperatives, NGOs) | Remainder | Variable |

**Source:** CGAP/Simon-Kucher Market Sizing 2021

### 3.2 Formal Credit Access (PRODUCE/SBS, 2024)

| Enterprise Size | SUNAT-Registered | With SF Access | Financial Inclusion Rate |
|----------------|-----------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Microempresa | 2,186,859 | 575,874 | **26.3%** |
| Pequeña empresa | 123,557 | 67,073 | **54.3%** |
| MYPE Total | 2,310,416 | 642,947 | **27.8%** |
| Mediana empresa | 5,155 | 3,096 | 60.1% |
| Gran empresa | 14,409 | 9,525 | 66.1% |

**Key gap:** Only 26.3% of formal microempresas access credit. The ~3.7 million informal MYPEs have essentially *zero* formal credit access.

### 3.3 Credit Portfolio Data (SBS/PRODUCE, 2024)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total system credit (2024) | S/ 213,121 million |
| MYPE credit total (2024) | S/ 68,054 million (31.9% of total) |
| — Microempresa credit | S/ 39,354 million |
| — Pequeña empresa credit | S/ 28,700 million |
| MYPE credit change (2024 vs 2023) | **−12.0%** |
| Average microempresa loan | S/ 68,000 (~USD $17,500) |
| Average pequeña empresa loan | S/ 428,000 (~USD $110,000) |
| MYPE deudores as % of total enterprise deudores | 99.6% |

**Critical trend:** MYPE credit *contracted* 12.0% in 2024 (from S/ 77,367M to S/ 68,054M), while corporate credit grew. The SBS confirmed MYPE credit was still contracting at −10.3% as of March 2025.

**Source:** PRODUCE Estado de la Inclusión Financiera MYPE Dec 2024; SBS Informe de Estabilidad 2024-I; microfinanzas.pe June 2025

### 3.4 Estimating the Credit Gap

Using CGAP's methodology and updated data:
- **Total MSE credit market (demand):** ~USD $23.7 billion
- **Formal credit supply to MYPEs (2024):** S/ 68,054M ≈ USD $17.5 billion
- **Estimated unmet demand:** ~USD $6–8 billion
- **Informal lending to MSEs:** ~USD $3.9 billion annually (family, moneylenders)
- **580,000+ urban families** borrowed from informal moneylenders/pawnshops in 2023
- **Average informal loan:** USD $461
- **49% of informal borrowers pay interest rates >500% annually** (Instituto Peruano de Economía)

**Source:** CGAP 2021; MDFin 2025; IPE 2023 via MDFin

### 4.6 Credinka — A Cautionary Tale

Financiera Credinka was intervened by SBS in September 2024 and its asset block transferred to Caja Arequipa. Key factors:
- Financial expense reached 45% of financial income
- Concentrated in Cusco (30%), Puno, Apurímac, Arequipa
- 95% of portfolio was micro and small enterprise
- Pandemic and post-pandemic deterioration accelerated capital erosion

**Lesson for Yaya:** Even specialized microfinance institutions with deep MYPE focus can fail when cost structures are misaligned. Digital-first, low-overhead models have structural advantages.

### 5.3 Geographic Concentration

Lima + Callao concentrates **87.6%** of total factoring volume (S/ 37,713M in 2024). This extreme centralization represents a massive opportunity for regional expansion.

### 5.4 Key Market Dynamics

- **Fondo CRECER (COFIDE):** Since 2024, covers up to 75% of impago in factoring operations with smaller pagadores
- **SUNAT/CAVALI infrastructure:** Electronic invoice (factura electrónica) registration enables digital factoring
- **Key platforms:** Prestamype (S/ 1.6B+ financed, 5,000+ active enterprises), Adelanta Factoring, Finsmart, Factorus
- **Growth driver:** PRODUCE projects 30%+ growth in 2025
- **Comparison:** Chile factoring = 16% of GDP; Spain = 26%; Peru = 4%

### 5.5 Barriers

1. **State not participating** as pagador (would double market volume)
2. **Confirming cerrado** — large companies force suppliers to factor only through specific entities
3. **Slow judicial enforcement** of unpaid facturas
4. **SUNAT role gap** — treats commercial invoice separately from título valor
5. **Regional concentration** — provinces underserved

**Source:** PRODUCE 2024; Gestión Feb 2025; BusinessEmpresarial Sep 2025

### 6.2 Interoperability Mandate

The BCRP mandated interoperability via Circular No. 0024-2022-BCRP:
- **Phase 1 (April 2023):** Yape ↔ Plin
- **Phase 2 (July 2023):** All digital wallets interconnected
- **Phase 3 (2024):** EEDEs and BIM integration

### 6.3 Yape's Financial Services Expansion

Yape has evolved beyond payments:
- **Microcredits** — offered directly through the app
- **Foreign exchange** — currency conversion
- **International remittances** — via TerraPay and EBANX partnerships
- **Yape Shop** — e-commerce marketplace
- **SUNAT integration** — fiscal tracking for businesses with >45,000 PEN annual revenue (from mid-2025)

### 6.4 Projected Growth

Digital wallets are projected to capture **28% of POS payments** and **31% of e-commerce payments** by 2027, with CAGR of 24%.

**Source:** PaymentsCMI 2025; BCRP

## 7. WhatsApp as Business Infrastructure

### 7.1 Penetration

- **97% of Peruvian entrepreneurs** use WhatsApp to sell, buy, and manage their business
- **98% penetration** among urban internet users
- **WhatsApp Business:** 1.2 million weekly active users in Peru (Q3 2024, Sensor Tower)
- **65,000 weekly downloads** of WhatsApp Business app

**Source:** Infobae Dec 2025; Sensor Tower via nteve.com

### 7.2 Usage Patterns

WhatsApp serves as the **de facto operating system** for Peruvian MYPEs:
- **Product catalog** distribution
- **Order management** and confirmations
- **Payment coordination** (Yape QR shared via WhatsApp is standard)
- **Customer service** and dispute resolution
- **Supplier relationships** and negotiation
- **Peer-to-peer business learning** groups

### 7.3 The WhatsApp-Yape Nexus

The dominant commercial flow for informal Peruvian micro-businesses:
1. Customer discovers product via WhatsApp (catalog, photos, voice notes)
2. Negotiation happens in WhatsApp chat
3. Payment via **Yape QR code shared in WhatsApp** or Plin transfer
4. Delivery coordination via WhatsApp
5. After-sales and reorder via WhatsApp

**This entire commercial lifecycle generates transaction data that is currently invisible to credit scoring systems.**

### 7.4 Conversational Banking

Financial institutions are beginning to integrate with WhatsApp:
- Account opening via WhatsApp chat
- Balance inquiries
- Loan applications
- AI-powered financial advisory

"With WhatsApp Business API, a PYME can reduce response time from 24 hours to instant and increase conversion by >25%" — Daniel Black, Treble

**Source:** Infobae Dec 2025; Emerging Fintech May 2025; AnswerForMe 2026

## 8. Financial Inclusion Initiatives

### 8.1 National Policy Framework

- **Política Nacional de Inclusión Financiera (PNIF):** Increased account ownership from 35% (2015) to 56% (2023)
- **Estrategia Nacional de Educación Financiera**
- **Plan Nacional de Competitividad y Productividad**

### 8.2 BIM (Billetera Móvil)

BIM was launched in February 2016 as the world's first interoperable mobile money platform:
- Operated by Pagos Digitales Peruanos (PDP)
- 25 electronic money issuers integrated
- Works on basic phones via USSD/SMS (no smartphone required)
- Only requires DNI to register
- **However:** BIM has been largely overtaken by Yape/Plin in adoption and usage
- BIM's Phase 3 interoperability with Yape/Plin was implemented in 2024
- Target audience: the ~10 million Peruvians with cellphones but no bank accounts

**Source:** Better Than Cash Alliance 2018; Center for Financial Inclusion 2017; BCRP

### 8.3 Fondo CRECER

- Managed by COFIDE (Peru's development finance institution)
- Provides guarantees to banks and MFIs for MSE credit
- Since 2024: covers up to 75% of default risk in factoring operations
- **Fintechs are NOT eligible** for Fondo CRECER guarantees (major policy gap)

## 9. Alternative Credit Scoring: The Transaction Data Opportunity

### 9.1 The Problem

Peru's credit infrastructure has critical blind spots:
- **SBS public credit bureau** (since 1968) only covers regulated lenders
- **Fintechs don't report to SBS** credit registry and can't freely access it
- **42% of adults** don't verify whether financial products come from regulated providers
- **91% of bodega owners** never received training on digital business tools (CGAP pilot)
- **Informal MSEs** take consumer loans or credit cards as individuals, masking business activity

### 9.2 Yape/Plin Transaction Data as Credit Signal

The massive adoption of digital wallets creates an unprecedented alternative data source:
- **17+ million Yape users** generating daily transaction histories
- **2+ million merchants** with payment flow records
- **1.6+ million daily interoperable transactions** between wallets
- **Average ticket size:** S/ 66.9 for interoperable transfers
- **SUNAT cross-referencing** (from mid-2025): businesses with >45,000 PEN annual Yape revenue are being tracked

**Potential credit scoring inputs from Yape/Plin data:**
1. **Transaction frequency** — regularity of income/sales
2. **Transaction value** — business volume proxy
3. **Customer diversity** — number of unique payers
4. **Seasonal patterns** — business cyclicality
5. **Payment behavior** — speed of B2B payments
6. **Network analysis** — supplier/customer relationship density
7. **Growth trajectory** — month-over-month revenue trends

### 9.3 Precedents

- **Kenya's Pezesha:** Proprietary credit scoring for SMEs using digital transaction data
- **India's UPI-based lending:** Transaction history from UPI payments used for instant loan approvals
- **Yape itself** already offers microcredits through the app, presumably using internal transaction data

### 9.4 Regulatory Pathway

- Open Banking framework in Peru is being discussed but not yet implemented
- BCRP's interoperability mandate creates standardized data flows
- SUNAT's fiscal oversight of Yape transactions creates government-validated revenue data
- SBS's Financial Inclusion Indicators report acknowledges the gap for informal MSEs

**Source:** CGAP 2023; BCRP; OECD/Indecopi 2025; UNDP Accelerator Labs

## 10. The Fintech Opportunity Gap

### 10.1 Why Fintechs Haven't Captured Market Share

CGAP's analysis identified several structural barriers:

1. **Fintechs hold only 0.3%** of Peru's MSE credit market
2. **Not included in government support programs** (Fondo CRECER, Reactiva Perú, FAE MYPE)
3. **Pay VAT on credit services** (incumbent MFIs don't)
4. **Limited access to payment clearing house (ACH)**
5. **Cannot freely access SBS debtor registry**
6. **Face mature, digitized competitors** (MiBanco, Caja Arequipa already on digital transformation paths)
7. **MSE digital literacy gap** — only 42% verify if providers are regulated

### 10.2 The Opportunity for Yaya Platform

The data reveals a massive, structurally underserved market:

| Segment | Size | Current Access | Opportunity |
|---------|------|---------------|-------------|
| Formal MYPEs without credit | ~1.67M enterprises | 0% formal credit | Alternative scoring via transaction data |
| Informal MYPEs | ~3.7M+ enterprises | Near-zero formal access | WhatsApp-native, trust-based approach |
| MYPEs paying >46% interest (banks) | 575,874 micro borrowers | Expensive formal credit | Lower-cost digital lending |
| Informal borrowers paying >500% | 580,000+ families | Exploitative informal credit | Any formal product is transformative |
| Regional MYPEs outside Lima | ~1.6M+ enterprises | Minimal fintech presence | Digital-first regional expansion |

**Total addressable credit gap:** Estimated USD $6–8 billion annually.

## 12. Strategic Implications for Yaya Platform

### The Core Thesis

Peru's microenterprise economy presents a **USD $6–8 billion annual credit gap** that existing institutions cannot close because:

1. **86.8% of MYPEs are informal** → invisible to traditional credit scoring
2. **Only 0.3% of MSE credit comes from fintechs** → massive whitespace
3. **97% of entrepreneurs already transact on WhatsApp** → built-in distribution channel
4. **17M+ Yape users generate credit-relevant transaction data** → alternative scoring inputs exist
5. **Factoring is growing 25–30% annually** but concentrated in Lima → regional opportunity
6. **Interest rates of 38–46% for microenterprise credit** → significant room for disruption
7. **Government programs exclude fintechs** → regulatory advocacy opportunity

### Where Yaya Can Win

**The intersection of WhatsApp (where business happens), Yape/Plin (where money moves), and alternative credit scoring (what the data reveals) is the precise opportunity space that no incumbent has yet occupied.**
