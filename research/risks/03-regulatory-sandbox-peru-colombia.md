## 1. What Are Regulatory Sandboxes and Why They Matter

A regulatory sandbox is a controlled testing environment established by a financial regulator that allows companies—both licensed financial institutions and, increasingly, unlicensed startups—to pilot innovative financial products and services under relaxed regulatory requirements, for a limited time, with a restricted number of users, and under the regulator's supervision.

The concept originated with the UK's Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) in 2015 and has since spread globally. In Latin America, regulatory sandboxes have become a critical mechanism for bridging the gap between fast-moving fintech innovation and slower-moving financial regulation.

**Why sandboxes matter for fintech/AI startups:**

- **Reduced regulatory uncertainty:** Startups can test whether their business model triggers licensing requirements before committing full resources to compliance.
- **Lower barriers to entry:** Temporary exemptions from certain regulations allow testing with real users without a full banking or financial services license.
- **Regulator education:** Sandboxes allow regulators to understand novel technologies (AI, blockchain, automated advisory) before crafting permanent rules.
- **Path to licensing:** Successful sandbox participation often provides a clearer route to obtaining permanent authorization.
- **Consumer protection:** Testing occurs under controlled conditions with safeguards, limiting risk to a small user base.
- **Market validation:** Sandbox participation signals regulatory legitimacy to investors, partners, and potential customers.

For an AI-powered WhatsApp business platform that touches financial services—automated invoicing, payment facilitation, micro-lending referrals, or AI-based financial advisory—understanding sandbox frameworks is essential to determine whether the platform requires regulatory authorization and how to obtain it efficiently.

## 2. Peru's Regulatory Sandbox Framework

### 2.1 Legal Foundation

Peru's regulatory sandbox has its legal basis in **Decreto de Urgencia N° 03-2020**, which amended the *Ley General del Sistema Financiero* (General Financial System Law) to grant the Superintendencia de Banca, Seguros y AFP (SBS) the authority to establish temporary testing environments for innovative financial models.

The SBS subsequently issued **Resolución SBS N° 2429-2021**, creating the *Reglamento para la realización temporal de actividades en modelos novedosos* (Regulation for the Temporary Performance of Activities in Novel Models)—Peru's first formal sandbox regulation.

### 2.2 The Original Sandbox (2021): A Cautionary Tale

The 2021 sandbox was structured around three testing regimes:

1. **General Regime:** No prior authorization required; for testing activities already contemplated in existing regulation.
2. **Flexibility Regime:** Requires SBS authorization; for testing activities that need temporary relaxation of one or more existing regulatory requirements.
3. **Extraordinary Regime:** Requires SBS authorization; for testing activities not contemplated in the current regulatory framework.

**Critical flaw:** The original sandbox was restricted exclusively to entities already licensed and supervised by the SBS—banks, insurance companies, pension fund administrators, and specialized financial companies. Startups, fintechs, and unlicensed entities were entirely excluded.

**Result:** As of late 2025, the sandbox had attracted only **one participant**: Banco de Crédito del Perú (BCP), Peru's largest commercial bank. BCP used the sandbox to launch **"CriptoCocos,"** a pilot project for buying and selling crypto-assets (Bitcoin and USDC). The legal community widely characterized the sandbox as a failure, with Chambers & Partners noting that "the regulator's main mistake was to restrict access to the sandbox to licensed entities, meaning that any FinTech or start-up faces an insurmountable bureaucratic wall."

### 2.3 The 2025 Reform: Opening the Doors

In November 2025, the SBS issued **Resolución SBS N° 04142-2025**, fundamentally overhauling the sandbox framework. Key changes:

- **Non-supervised entities now eligible:** Fintechs, startups, and technology companies can now apply to the sandbox, provided they are formally constituted businesses.
- **Expanded participant base:** Cooperatives of savings and credit (*cooperativas de ahorro y crédito*), AFOCAT (mandatory traffic accident insurance administrators), and other previously excluded entities are now admitted.
- **Dual entry pathways:** Companies can enter either through (a) open calls (*convocatorias*) initiated by the SBS itself, or (b) formal applications submitted by interested companies.
- **Duration:** Pilot projects can last up to **18 months**, renewable for an additional **12 months**, for a total maximum of **30 months** (2.5 years).
- **Proactive regulator role:** The SBS has shifted from passive supervisor to active innovation promoter, including launching its own calls for specific innovation themes.
- **Requirements maintained:** Participants must demonstrate formal business constitution, cybersecurity policies, exit plans, and consumer protection guarantees.

**Important caveat:** Participation in the sandbox does not constitute authorization to operate within SBS-supervised systems. After the sandbox period, companies must either obtain proper licensing or cease the tested activities.

### 2.4 Current Status (Early 2026)

The reformed sandbox is in its early implementation phase. No public cohort results have been announced yet under the new framework, but the regulatory infrastructure is in place. The fintech community has received the reform positively, viewing it as a "key milestone" for loan companies, currency exchange companies, factoring companies, and other fintechs.

Peru's broader fintech environment includes:
- **Yape** (BCP): 17+ million users, Peru's dominant digital wallet
- **Plin** (BBVA/Scotiabank/Interbank): 13+ million users
- **132 million interoperable transactions** processed between Yape and Plin (June 2024–June 2025)
- **BCRP interoperability mandate** successfully implemented in phases (2023–2024)
- **100+ factoring companies** registered—a bright spot in Peru's fintech ecosystem
- Only **2 fully operational crowdfunding platforms** (Inversiones.io and Prestopolis)

## 3. Colombia's Regulatory Sandbox Framework

### 3.1 Dual Sandbox Architecture

Colombia uniquely operates **two distinct sandbox mechanisms**, both managed by the Superintendencia Financiera de Colombia (SFC):

#### LaArenera (Supervision Sandbox) — Established 2018

- **Purpose:** Testing financial innovations that require exemption from SFC-issued instructions only.
- **Who can apply:** Supervised entities (banks, insurers, etc.) directly, or non-supervised companies in alliance with a supervised entity.
- **Entry process:** Initial approach through *elHub* (SFC's innovation contact point), followed by formal application via the *Formato de solicitud de servicios de laArenera*.
- **Track record:** Multiple projects approved and completed since 2019.

#### Espacio Controlado de Prueba (Regulatory Sandbox) — Established via Decreto 1234 de 2020

- **Purpose:** Testing innovations that require exemption from regulations issued by the SFC **or other authorities** (broader scope than laArenera).
- **Who can apply:**
  - Non-supervised companies seeking to perform activities proper to supervised entities
  - Supervised entities seeking to perform activities outside their license
  - Supervised entities innovating within their licensed activities
- **Legal framework:** Decreto 2555 de 2010, Circular Externa 006 de 2025 (Part I, Title I, Chapter VIII), and the manual published by SFC.
- **Restrictions:** Participants cannot be exempted from data protection laws (Ley 1581/2012, Ley 1266/2008), foreign exchange and tax regulations, or Banco de la República monetary/credit regulations.

### 3.2 Notable Sandbox Participants and Outcomes

Colombia's sandboxes have been significantly more active than Peru's. Key projects:

| Participant | Scope | Period | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| UALET / AFIN Comisionista de Bolsa | Simplified onboarding for collective investment funds (replicating CATS model) | Feb 2019 – Oct 2019 | Led to Circular Externa 027/2020 |
| Asobancaria | Promoting and dynamizing digital payments | Sep 2019 – Sep 2020 | Informed regulatory updates |
| Santander Caceis / Precia / CML Analytics | DLT-based collateral management for OTC derivatives | Sep 2020 – Dec 2020 | Completed proof of concept |
| Seguros SURA | Personalized, event-triggered insurance products | Nov 2019 – Feb 2021 | Insurance innovation framework |
| SBS Seguros Colombia | Telematics-based "pay-per-kilometer" auto insurance | Jun 2020 – Dec 2021 | Simplified onboarding validated |
| Davivienda / BID Invest / Banco de la República | DLT-based bond issuance and OTC trading | Jun 2021 – Aug 2022 | Successful proof of concept |
| MOVII + Bitpoint Colombia | Crypto cash-in/cash-out through e-money accounts | Jun 2021 – Dec 2023 | Extended operations |

### 3.3 Colombia's Broader Fintech Context

- **394 fintech startups** active as of Q1 2024, with 5.8% growth
- **Nequi** (Bancolombia subsidiary): 20+ million users, fully digital bank
- **RappiPay:** Payment solutions integrated with Rappi's delivery ecosystem
- **Bre-B:** Interoperable instant payments system under development (modeled after Brazil's Pix)
- **Open Finance framework** (Decreto 1297/2022): Among the most advanced in Latin America
- **Bill 510 of 2024:** Proposed legislation clarifying rules for Virtual Asset Service Providers
- **36.3% investment growth** in 2024—fastest among major LatAm markets
- **410+ active fintech companies** as of 2025

## 4. Peru vs. Colombia: Comparative Analysis

| Dimension | Peru | Colombia |
|---|---|---|
| **Sandbox maturity** | Early stage (reformed Nov 2025) | Mature (operational since 2018) |
| **Number of sandbox programs** | 1 (SBS sandbox) | 2 (laArenera + Espacio Controlado de Prueba) |
| **Access for non-licensed entities** | Yes (since Nov 2025 reform) | Yes (in alliance with supervised entity for laArenera; independently for Espacio Controlado) |
| **Total sandbox participants to date** | 1 (BCP only, under old rules) | 15+ projects completed or active |
| **Maximum test duration** | 30 months (18 + 12 renewal) | Varies by project; typically 12–24 months |
| **Regulatory innovation hub** | No dedicated hub | elHub (dedicated innovation gateway) |
| **Dedicated fintech law** | No | No (but comprehensive decree-based framework) |
| **Interoperability mandate** | Yes (BCRP, implemented 2023) | In progress (Bre-B launching 2025–2026) |
| **Crypto sandbox activity** | 1 project (BCP CriptoCocos) | 7+ crypto cash-in/cash-out projects |
| **Open Finance framework** | Under discussion (proposed bill) | Active (Decreto 1297/2022) |
| **Ease of entry for startups** | Improving but unproven | Moderate (requires alliance with supervised entity for laArenera) |
| **Regulator posture** | Becoming proactive | Established innovation champion |

**Key takeaway:** Colombia has a 7-year head start on Peru in sandbox implementation and has built institutional capacity (elHub, published manuals, checklists, circular updates) that Peru is only beginning to develop. However, Peru's 2025 reform is ambitious in its scope—directly admitting non-supervised entities without requiring partnership with a bank—which could, if well-implemented, leapfrog Colombia's alliance requirement for laArenera.

## 5. How a WhatsApp-Based AI Platform Could Qualify

### 5.1 Determining Regulatory Scope

A WhatsApp-based AI business platform's sandbox eligibility depends on which specific financial activities it performs:

| Platform Feature | Regulatory Trigger | Sandbox Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Automated invoicing** | Generally not a regulated financial activity | Low — may not need sandbox |
| **Payment processing/facilitation** | BCRP registration (Peru); SFC oversight if collecting funds (Colombia) | Medium — sandbox useful for novel payment models |
| **Micro-lending facilitation** | If facilitating loans: SBS registration required (Peru); fundraising restrictions apply (Colombia) | High — ideal sandbox use case |
| **AI financial advisory** | Potential securities regulation if providing investment advice | High — robo-advice tested in Colombia's sandbox |
| **Currency exchange** | SBS registration (Peru); regulated activity (Colombia) | Medium-High |
| **Factoring/invoice financing** | SBS registration above PEN 800M threshold (Peru); regulated (Colombia) | Medium |

### 5.2 Eligible Sandbox Use Cases

**Peru (under new framework):**
- AI-powered credit scoring for micro-lending decisions via WhatsApp
- Automated financial health assessments delivered conversationally
- WhatsApp-based payment facilitation using novel UX patterns
- AI-driven factoring recommendations for SME suppliers
- Conversational insurance product distribution

**Colombia (via laArenera or Espacio Controlado):**
- AI chatbot for personalized financial advisory (robo-advice has precedent via Decreto 661/2018)
- WhatsApp-integrated payment processing via Bre-B rails
- Micro-insurance distribution through conversational AI
- Stablecoin-based cross-border payment facilitation for SMEs
- Automated KYC/onboarding via WhatsApp for financial products

### 5.3 Strategic Positioning

The platform should position itself not as a financial institution but as a **technology enabler** that:
1. Partners with licensed entities for regulated activities
2. Seeks sandbox participation for novel AI-driven financial features
3. Maintains core business operations (invoicing, business management) outside regulatory scope
4. Gradually builds toward independent financial services capabilities

## 6. Application Process and Requirements

### 6.1 Peru — SBS Sandbox Application

**Step 1: Pre-assessment**
- Determine whether the intended activity falls within SBS regulatory scope
- Identify which sandbox regime applies (General, Flexibility, or Extraordinary)

**Step 2: Formal Application**
- Submit application during an SBS convocatoria (open call) or via independent formal request
- Required documentation:
  - Company formation documents (must be formally constituted)
  - Detailed description of the innovative model
  - Risk analysis and mitigation plan
  - Consumer protection measures
  - Cybersecurity policies
  - Exit plan (how to wind down if pilot fails or concludes)
  - Guarantee/bond as required by SBS

**Step 3: SBS Review**
- SBS evaluates whether the proposal adds value to user experience or contributes to industry development
- Timeline: Not yet established under new framework; expect 3–6 months based on regional norms

**Step 4: Pilot Execution**
- 6-month startup window after approval
- Up to 18 months of active testing
- 12-month extension available if justified
- Periodic reporting to SBS

**Step 5: Post-Sandbox**
- Apply for permanent authorization if successful
- Cease tested activities if authorization is not obtained

### 6.2 Colombia — SFC Sandbox Application

**Step 1: Approach via elHub**
- Initial consultation through elHub (SFC's innovation gateway)
- Complete the *Formato de solicitud de servicios de elHub*
- elHub team will help determine whether laArenera or Espacio Controlado de Prueba is appropriate

**Step 2: Eligibility Check**
- Complete the *Lista de chequeo verificación de requisitos para ingreso*
- For laArenera: non-supervised entities must present in alliance with a supervised entity
- For Espacio Controlado: broader access but stricter documentation

**Step 3: Formal Application**
- Submit detailed proposal covering:
  - Business model description
  - Innovation component (must use new/emerging technology or apply existing technology in novel ways)
  - Consumer benefit analysis
  - Risk management framework
  - Data protection compliance (Ley 1581/2012 and Ley 1266/2008 are non-negotiable)
  - Tax and foreign exchange compliance plan

**Step 4: Certificate of Temporary Operation**
- If approved, SFC issues a *Certificado de Operación Temporal*
- Complete the *Lista de chequeo Constitución de entidades vigiladas en el Espacio Controlado de Prueba*

**Step 5: Testing and Reporting**
- Operate under defined parameters
- Regular reporting to SFC
- Consumer complaints mechanism must be active

**Step 6: Transition**
- Demonstrate regulatory compliance for permanent licensing, or
- Wind down operations

## 7. Regional Context: Latin American Sandbox Landscape

### 7.1 Mexico — Ley Fintech (2018)

Mexico pioneered fintech regulation in Latin America with the *Ley para Regular las Instituciones de Tecnología Financiera* (March 2018). Its sandbox component, "modelos novedosos," allows temporary authorization for up to **2 years plus 1-year extension** (3 years total). However, only **6 startups** have been selected via the CNBV's Sandbox Challenge. Post-sandbox transition to full regulatory compliance has been criticized as overly burdensome. Mexico's National Digital Finance Strategy (2025–2030) aims to modernize and make the framework more flexible. The country has 1,100+ fintech companies and 70+ million fintech users.

### 7.2 Brazil — BCB Sandbox

Brazil operates **three separate sandboxes** for financial systems (Banco Central do Brasil), capital markets (CVM), and insurance (SUSEP). The BCB sandbox admits both authorized and unauthorized institutions, with pilot periods of **1 year plus 1-year extension**. Brazil's fintech ecosystem is the region's largest, powered by Pix (42 billion transactions in 2023, 95% population adoption) and Open Finance (42 million active user consents, 1.5 billion weekly API calls). Nubank has surpassed 100 million customers in Brazil alone. The Virtual Assets Law (BVAL, 2022–2025) positioned BCB as crypto regulator.

### 7.3 Chile — CMF Approach

Chile took a contrarian position, initially arguing a sandbox was unnecessary because its regulatory framework did not restrict fintech innovation. This changed with **Ley N° 21,521** (Chile's Fintech Act), which created a more structured framework. The CMF has moved toward Open Finance experimentation but has not implemented a traditional sandbox model. Chile's approach emphasizes regulation through existing frameworks rather than temporary exemptions.

### 7.4 Regional Trends

- **Convergence toward open sandboxes:** Most countries are moving to admit non-licensed entities
- **Crypto as sandbox catalyst:** Crypto cash-in/cash-out has been the most popular sandbox use case across the region
- **Real-time payments integration:** Pix (Brazil), SPEI (Mexico), Bre-B (Colombia) are creating new opportunities for sandbox innovation
- **AI regulation gap:** No Latin American country has AI-specific financial regulation as of early 2026, creating both opportunity and uncertainty
- **IDB advocacy:** The Inter-American Development Bank has been a strong advocate for regional sandbox convergence and cross-border testing agreements

## 8. Risks and Limitations of Sandbox Programs

### 8.1 Structural Risks

- **No guarantee of permanent licensing:** Sandbox participation does not create any right or expectation of receiving a permanent license. In Peru specifically, "participation does not constitute authorization to operate within systems supervised by the SBS."
- **Post-sandbox cliff:** The transition from relaxed sandbox rules to full regulatory compliance can be devastating for startups, as Mexico's experience demonstrates. Companies may need to meet capital requirements, governance standards, and compliance infrastructure that far exceed their sandbox-stage capacity.
- **Regulatory uncertainty during participation:** Rules can change mid-pilot. Regulatory circulars, new decrees, or changes in political leadership can alter the sandbox terms.

### 8.2 Operational Risks

- **Limited user base:** Sandbox testing with restricted users may not provide statistically meaningful data for AI/ML models that require large datasets for training and validation.
- **Time constraints:** 18–30 months may be insufficient for complex AI systems to demonstrate value, especially in lending where loan performance takes months to observe.
- **Partner dependency (Colombia):** Requiring alliance with a supervised entity for laArenera means the startup's fate is partially tied to the partner bank's strategic decisions.
- **Resource drain:** Sandbox applications require significant legal, compliance, and documentation resources that compete with product development.

### 8.3 Market Risks

- **Sandbox washing:** Some companies use sandbox participation primarily for marketing legitimacy without genuine intention to innovate.
- **Competitive exposure:** Detailed proposals shared with regulators may be visible to incumbents who sit on advisory boards or have regulatory relationships.
- **Regional fragmentation:** A sandbox approval in Peru has no validity in Colombia and vice versa. Cross-border expansion requires separate applications in each jurisdiction.

### 8.4 AI-Specific Risks

- **Algorithmic accountability:** Regulators increasingly expect explainable AI, which may conflict with complex ML models used for credit scoring or financial advisory.
- **Data privacy:** Both Peru and Colombia have data protection laws that apply even within sandboxes. AI training on financial data requires careful consent architecture.
- **Bias and fairness:** AI-driven financial services must demonstrate non-discriminatory outcomes, a growing concern for Latin American regulators focused on financial inclusion.

## 9. Strategic Recommendations: Peru-First, Colombia-Second Expansion

### 9.1 Phase 1: Peru Market Entry (Months 1–12)

1. **Launch core platform without sandbox:** Automated invoicing, business management, and non-financial WhatsApp features do not require sandbox participation. Go to market immediately.

2. **Register with relevant authorities:** If the platform facilitates payments or lending, register with the appropriate entity (SBS for lending-adjacent activities, BCRP for payment services).

3. **Apply to SBS sandbox:** Once the reformed sandbox accepts applications, submit a proposal for the AI-driven financial features (micro-lending facilitation, AI financial advisory). Target the Flexibility or Extraordinary regime.

4. **Build factoring partnerships:** Peru's factoring ecosystem (100+ registered companies) offers a low-regulatory-burden path to financial services. Consider integrating factoring marketplace features into the WhatsApp platform.

5. **Engage with BCRP interoperability framework:** Position the platform for Phase 4 integration (fintechs/big techs) of Peru's retail payments interoperability strategy.

### 9.2 Phase 2: Colombia Expansion (Months 12–24)

1. **Establish Colombian entity:** Incorporate a local company or establish a branch.

2. **Engage elHub immediately:** Begin conversations with SFC's innovation team before formal application. This pre-engagement is explicitly recommended by SFC and can shape your application strategy.

3. **Identify supervised entity partner:** For laArenera access, secure an alliance with a bank or financial institution. Consider Bancolombia (innovation-forward, launched Nequi and Wenia), Davivienda (active sandbox participant), or a SEDPE (Sociedad Especializada en Depósitos y Pagos Electrónicos) like MOVII.

4. **Apply to Espacio Controlado de Prueba:** If the AI platform model doesn't fit neatly into existing regulated categories, the regulatory sandbox's broader scope may be more appropriate.

5. **Integrate with Bre-B rails:** Colombia's upcoming instant payment system will create new opportunities for WhatsApp-based payment features.

### 9.3 Cross-Cutting Recommendations

- **Document everything for regulators:** Latin American regulators respond well to comprehensive, well-structured proposals. Invest in regulatory documentation as a core competency.
- **Hire local regulatory counsel:** In both countries, relationships with regulators matter enormously. Local lawyers with SBS/SFC relationships are essential.
- **Design for explainability:** Build AI models that can be explained to non-technical regulators. This is both a regulatory necessity and a competitive advantage.
- **Consumer protection first:** Both countries prioritize consumer protection. Build complaint mechanisms, clear disclosures, and data rights into the platform from day one.
- **Track regional regulatory developments:** Mexico's Ley Fintech reforms, Brazil's Open Finance evolution, and Chile's Fintech Act all influence Peru and Colombia's regulatory thinking.
