# A Day in the Life: How Peru's Micro-Enterprise Owners Actually Run Their Businesses

**Classification:** Market — Ethnographic User Research & Product Design Implications  
**Date:** March 21, 2026  
**Sources:** DAI/Ipsos Peru MSME Survey (997 respondents, 2021), ILO Lima Informal Micro-Entrepreneurs Study (67 interviews, Cono Norte), MIT Global SCALE Capstone (63 companies across 8 LATAM countries, 2016-2017), IDB Small Business Justice Study (Peru), ProAcceso LATAM SME Challenges Report (2025), University of Essex Peru Micro-Enterprise Panel Data (2,471-2,231 firms, 1994-2000)  

---

## Executive Summary

Understanding how Peru's micro-enterprise owners actually spend their days is essential for designing a product they'll use. This document synthesizes field research, surveys, and ethnographic studies to construct a composite "day in the life" of Yaya's target user — painting a picture that goes far beyond demographic data into behavioral reality.

**Core insight from MIT SCALE research:** Business owners spend **86% of their time on operative activities** — sweeping, cleaning, opening doors, serving customers, and other hands-on work. They are afraid to delegate and they multi-task constantly. This means Yaya must integrate into the flow of operational work, not add another screen to check. The voice-first, conversational approach isn't a nice-to-have — it's the only viable interface for someone whose hands are busy 86% of the day.

### 1.2 The Owner-Operator Reality

The most critical finding across all studies: **the owner IS the business.** There is no management layer, no back office, no IT department. The owner does everything:

From MIT SCALE research:
> "Students confirmed that business owners spend **86% of their time in operative activities** including sweeping, cleaning, opening the doors, etc. Owners are afraid to delegate and they multi-task."

From ILO Lima study:
> "Most micro-entrepreneurs, regardless of the activity they run, are responsible for **management as well as for distribution, purchases of inputs, and production.** This limited division of labour is consistent with the characteristics of informal enterprises."

## 2. A Composite Day in the Life

### 2.1 Rosa: Salon Owner in San Juan de Lurigancho, Lima

**Profile:** 38 years old, 2 employees (one paid, one daughter), 12 years in business, registered as Persona Natural, WhatsApp Business user, accepts Yape payments.

**6:30 AM** — Wakes up, checks WhatsApp. 14 unread messages from customers asking about availability. Responds to 8, the others she'll get to later. Makes mental note of who wants what service.

**7:00 AM** — Opens the salon. Sweeps, sets up stations, turns on water heater. Checks supplies: shampoo running low, need to buy from distributor. Writes this on a scrap of paper.

**7:30 AM** — First customer arrives (walk-in). Rosa does the haircut herself while her daughter handles the cash — a S/30 payment via Yape. The daughter screenshot the Yape confirmation. No receipt issued.

**8:00-12:00 PM** — Continuous stream of customers. Between cuts, Rosa:
- Responds to WhatsApp messages (scheduling, price questions)
- Scrolls customer photos on her phone to remember preferences
- Tracks payments mentally or on a torn notebook page
- Answers phone calls from suppliers
- Sends voice notes to customers confirming appointments
- Takes 3 Yape payments and 5 cash payments

**The notebook problem:** Rosa's "accounting system" is a notebook where she writes total daily sales. Individual transactions aren't tracked. She doesn't know her most popular service, her profit margins, or which customers are most frequent. She does her monthly SUNAT filing by adding up notebook pages — often with errors.

**12:00-1:00 PM** — Lunch break. Eats at the salon. Catches up on WhatsApp messages. Has 22 unread messages now. Responds to the urgent ones, marks others as unread (which she'll forget about).

**1:00-6:00 PM** — Afternoon rush. More cuts, more Yape payments, more WhatsApp juggling. A supplier calls — the shampoo delivery will be tomorrow. Rosa agrees but forgets to update her mental inventory tracking.

**A missed appointment:** A customer who booked via WhatsApp voice note yesterday shows up — but Rosa doesn't remember the booking and has no slot available. Customer is annoyed. Rosa apologizes and offers a discount next time (which she'll also forget about).

**6:00 PM** — Last customer leaves. Rosa counts the cash drawer: S/380. Plus S/180 in Yape payments she can see in the app. Total day: ~S/560. She writes "560" in her notebook. She doesn't track expenses incurred today.

**7:00 PM** — Goes home. Checks WhatsApp one more time. 11 messages about tomorrow. Responds to 6, too tired for the rest.

**What Rosa doesn't know at the end of this day:**
- Her exact profit (she knows revenue but not costs)
- Which services were most popular
- How many customers she lost to missed WhatsApp messages
- What her inventory status actually is
- Whether she's on track for her monthly SUNAT obligations
- Which customers are overdue for repeat visits

### 2.2 Carlos: Bodega Owner in Villa El Salvador, Lima

**Profile:** 45 years old, wife helps in the store, 15 years in business, operates from the front of his home, Régimen MYPE Tributario, accepts cash and Yape.

**5:30 AM** — Opens the bodega. Pulls metal shutters, arranges produce on the sidewalk display. Checks which products sold yesterday by looking at empty spots on shelves.

**6:00-10:00 AM** — Morning rush. Breakfast items, bread, eggs, milk. Carlos handles all sales while his wife restocks. Transactions are:
- Cash (70%): No record kept. Carlos makes change from a metal box.
- Yape (30%): Screenshot of confirmation. Amount visible in app.

**The pricing problem:** Carlos adjusts prices mentally based on what he paid the distributor. When costs change (frequent with Peru's inflation), he sometimes forgets to update certain items. He has no system for tracking cost vs. sale price per product.

**10:00 AM** — Distributor arrives. Carlos orders by looking at his shelves: "Dame 5 cajas de leche, 3 de arroz, 10 de gaseosas." He pays cash from the register — no receipt requested (informal distributor). No record of the purchase.

**10:00 AM-1:00 PM** — Steady stream of neighbors. Carlos knows regulars by name. Some have credit ("fío") — they buy now and pay later. Carlos tracks fío in his head or on a list behind the counter. Sometimes he forgets who owes what.

**The credit problem:** Carlos estimates that S/2,000-3,000 in fío is outstanding at any time. He has no systematic way to track it, remind customers, or know which credit relationships are profitable vs. risky.

**1:00-7:00 PM** — Afternoon/evening sales. Lower traffic but steady. Carlos's wife takes over while he naps briefly, then returns. No handoff of what sold — they "just know."

**7:00 PM** — Close. Carlos counts cash: S/420 today. Plus S/130 Yape. Total: S/550. Expenses: he remembers spending S/300 at the distributor. Rough profit: S/250. He writes nothing down — carries these numbers in his head.

**What Carlos doesn't know:**
- Actual profit margin by product category
- Total outstanding fío and who owes what
- Which products are moving fast vs. slow
- When he should reorder (vs. when he runs out)
- His effective cash flow position
- Whether his pricing covers his actual costs

## 3. The Seven Pain Points Yaya Must Solve

### 3.1 Pain Point #1: The WhatsApp Inbox Overwhelm

**Finding (DAI/Ipsos):** 55% of online MSMEs use WhatsApp to communicate with customers. But the tool creates its own problems:
- Messages get buried in the feed
- No way to distinguish appointment requests from casual messages
- Voice notes must be listened to entirely to know the content
- No automatic scheduling or confirmation
- No search capability for customer history

**Yaya solution:** Conversational AI that automatically classifies, schedules, and confirms appointments from WhatsApp messages. The owner says "confirma todas las citas de mañana" and Yaya sends confirmation messages to each booked customer.

### 3.2 Pain Point #2: The Paper Notebook Accounting

**Finding (MIT SCALE):** Only 55% of micro-enterprises adopt any supply chain practices. Financial tracking is the area with the most room for improvement. Most keep records in notebooks — if at all.

**The cascade of notebook problems:**
1. Notebooks get lost or damaged
2. Handwriting is illegible
3. No automatic calculations
4. Can't query historical data ("how much did I sell last March?")
5. Monthly SUNAT filing requires manually reconstructing from notebooks
6. No backup if the notebook is stolen

**Yaya solution:** Voice-based transaction recording. "Vendí un corte por cincuenta soles" → automatically recorded, categorized, and available for SUNAT filing. No notebook required.

### 3.3 Pain Point #3: The Informal Credit (Fío) Black Hole

**Finding (IDB Peru study):** Businesses rely on reputation and trust factors rather than formal contracts. This extends to customer credit, which is tracked informally and often forgotten.

**Yaya solution:** "Registra que María me debe cien soles" → tracked, reminders sent before due date, aging report available by voice query.

### 3.4 Pain Point #4: The Inventory Guessing Game

**Finding (MIT SCALE):** The majority of companies do not have an inventory model. Reordering is based on visual inspection of shelves.

**Yaya solution:** Voice-based inventory updates triggered by sales and purchases. "Compré cinco cajas de shampoo" + "Vendí tres shampoos hoy" → Yaya knows current stock and can alert when levels are low.

### 3.5 Pain Point #5: The SUNAT Compliance Burden

**Finding (ProAcceso):** In Peru, high compliance costs and complex procedures push 56-80% of micro-enterprises into informality. The perceived value proposition for formalization is weak.

**Yaya opportunity:** If Yaya makes SUNAT compliance effortless (automatic invoice generation, monthly filing preparation, tax obligation tracking), it flips the cost-benefit calculation of formalization. The compliance burden goes from "hours per month of manual paperwork" to "Yaya does it automatically."

### 3.6 Pain Point #6: The 86% Operative Time Problem

**Finding (MIT SCALE):** Owners spend 86% of time on operative activities. They can't stop cutting hair or serving customers to type into an app.

**Yaya solution:** Voice-first, hands-free operation. Record a sale while blow-drying hair. Check inventory while stocking shelves. Review daily numbers while locking up.

### 3.7 Pain Point #7: The Trust Deficit

**Finding (IDB Peru study):** Businesses view the judicial system as "corrupt, slow, complex and expensive." They avoid contracts, new suppliers, and growth because they can't enforce agreements. They rely on personal relationships as their primary protection.

**Yaya opportunity:** Build trust through daily proven reliability. If Yaya accurately tracks S/50 sales today, the owner will trust it with S/5,000 expense tracking tomorrow. Trust compounds over time — exactly matching Yaya's five-layer moat strategy.

## 5. The Management Capability Gap

### 5.1 What Owners Are Good At (and Bad At)

**Strong capabilities:**
- Customer relationships (repeat business through personal connection)
- Product/service delivery (hands-on expertise)
- Improvisation and resilience
- Family coordination

**Weak capabilities (from MIT SCALE and ProAcceso):**
- Financial management (biggest gap)
- Inventory management (no models or systems)
- Strategic planning (short-term survival focus)
- Marketing beyond word-of-mouth
- Record-keeping and compliance

### 5.2 Efficiency Differences

From MIT SCALE across LATAM:
- **Women are 5 points more efficient** than men in operations
- **Non-family companies are more efficient** (69%) than family-owned (66%)
- **More employees → more efficiency** (through delegation)
- **Young owners (<35) have higher education** but are less efficient with costs
- **Older owners (>35) are more efficient** with costs but less educated

### 5.3 The Delegation Fear

The MIT SCALE finding that owners are "afraid to delegate" is crucial for Yaya:
- They don't delegate because they don't trust employees with financial information
- They don't delegate because there's no system to track what employees do
- They don't delegate because the "system" is in their head

**Yaya as delegation enabler:** If Yaya tracks all transactions and the owner can query at any time ("¿Cuánto vendió mi empleada hoy?"), the fear of delegation diminishes. Yaya becomes the transparent management layer that lets owners step back from 86% operative time.

## 6. The Trust & Credit Economy

### 6.1 How Trust Works in Peruvian Micro-Enterprise

From IDB Peru study:
> "In order to lessen the risks of noncompliance, businesses rely on reputation and trust factors. They avoid changing suppliers, even if this means higher production costs, unless they are able to investigate the credit and business record of potential new suppliers."

This creates:
- **Supplier lock-in:** Owners pay more rather than risk untrusted new suppliers
- **Customer credit (fío) based on personal knowledge:** No formal system
- **Reluctance to grow:** New customers/suppliers = unknown trust
- **Cash preference:** Cash requires no trust in financial systems

### 6.2 Implications for Yaya's Trust Architecture

Yaya must earn trust the same way suppliers and customers do — through repeated reliable interactions:

1. **Start small:** Track a few sales correctly
2. **Prove reliability:** Never lose data, never record wrong amounts
3. **Build incrementally:** Once trust is established for sales, extend to expenses, then inventory, then SUNAT
4. **Never break trust:** A single data loss or wrong amount could permanently damage the relationship

The accountant trust transfer chain (documented in market/03) becomes even more important in this context — a recommendation from a trusted accountant carries more weight than any marketing.

## 7. Product Design Implications

### 7.1 Interface Requirements

| Requirement | Rationale | Implementation |
|---|---|---|
| **Voice-first** | 86% operative time; hands busy | WhatsApp voice notes as primary input |
| **No learning curve** | "Lack of knowledge" is #1 barrier | Natural conversation, not commands |
| **Works offline** | 39% cite connectivity issues | Queue messages, sync when connected |
| **Immediate value** | Must prove worth on Day 1 | First value = respond to a WhatsApp message automatically |
| **No app install** | WhatsApp is already installed | Pure WhatsApp integration |
| **Bilingual flexibility** | Some code-switching to English/Quechua | Multi-code STT processing |
| **Patient & forgiving** | Users will make mistakes | "No entendí, ¿puedes repetir?" without judgment |

### 7.2 Onboarding Flow

Based on user behavior research, onboarding must:

1. **Take <5 minutes** — the owner is working
2. **Start with one value proposition** — "¿Quieres que registre tus ventas por voz?"
3. **Demonstrate immediately** — "Dime una venta que hiciste hoy"
4. **Confirm and celebrate** — "Listo, registré S/50 por un corte de pelo. Así de fácil."
5. **Add features gradually** — Don't explain everything at once
6. **Use voice for onboarding** — Don't send walls of text

### 7.3 Error Handling

The system must handle common user patterns gracefully:
- **Interrupted mid-sentence:** "Vendí un cor— perdón, ya vengo" → pause and resume
- **Wrong amount corrected:** "No, fueron sesenta, no cincuenta" → correct without frustration
- **Vague requests:** "¿Cómo vamos?" → interpret as daily sales summary
- **Mixed voice/text:** Owner switches between typing and voice notes freely
- **Group chat confusion:** Owner has the salon group and Yaya in the same WhatsApp; messages must be routed correctly

## 8. Key Takeaways for Andre

1. **86% operative time is the fundamental constraint.** Any solution that requires the owner to stop working and use an app will fail. Voice-first, hands-free operation isn't a feature — it's an architectural requirement.

2. **The notebook-to-Yaya transition is the core product moment.** The day Rosa stops writing "560" in her notebook and instead tells Yaya about each sale is the day she gains real financial visibility. Make this transition feel effortless.

3. **Trust builds slowly but breaks instantly.** In Peru's low-trust environment, Yaya must earn credibility through consistent accuracy over weeks before the owner will rely on it for SUNAT compliance. One data loss = game over.

4. **Fío (informal credit) tracking is a killer feature.** Owners have S/2,000-3,000 in untracked customer credit. Giving them visibility into this — and gentle reminder capabilities — could immediately improve cash flow and demonstrate Yaya's value.

5. **"Support, don't replace" is the correct design philosophy.** The DAI/Ipsos research is explicit: digital tools must augment existing workflows. Yaya should slide into the WhatsApp conversation flow, not demand a new workflow.

6. **Women-owned businesses are efficiency-first adopters.** With 40% female ownership and 5-point higher efficiency scores, women micro-entrepreneurs may be the most receptive early adopters — they already optimize more than their male counterparts.

7. **The delegation enabler story is powerful.** If Yaya can help owners move from 86% operative time to 70% by providing transparent tracking that enables delegation, it's not just an accounting tool — it's a growth enabler.
