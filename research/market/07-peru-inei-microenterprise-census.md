## 1. INEI: Peru's Census and Statistical Authority

The **Instituto Nacional de Estadística e Informática (INEI)** is Peru's governing body of the National Statistical System (*Sistema Estadístico Nacional*). Established as an autonomous technical entity, INEI is responsible for producing, analyzing, and disseminating official statistical information across all dimensions of Peru's economy and society. For the microenterprise landscape, INEI fulfills three critical functions:

1. **Business Demography** — Through its *Directorio Central de Empresas y Establecimientos* (DCEE), updated quarterly using SUNAT's taxpayer registry, INEI tracks the creation, reactivation, and closure of businesses nationwide. The quarterly *Demografía Empresarial en el Perú* bulletin is the definitive source for enterprise stock and flows.

2. **Household Surveys** — The *Encuesta Nacional de Hogares* (ENAHO), a continuous survey of approximately 40,000 households annually, captures employment, income, business ownership, and socioeconomic conditions. ENAHO is the primary instrument for measuring the informal economy and the characteristics of micro-enterprise owners.

3. **ICT Statistics** — INEI publishes quarterly reports on information and communication technology adoption in households, derived from ENAHO modules, providing critical data on internet access, mobile phone ownership, and digital tool usage.

Additionally, the **Ministerio de la Producción (PRODUCE)** publishes the annual *Las MIPYME en Cifras* report, synthesizing SUNAT administrative records and ENAHO data to provide the most comprehensive picture of formally registered micro, small, and medium enterprises. The most recent edition covers 2023 data, published in October 2024.

## 2. Key Surveys and Data Sources

### 2.1 Encuesta Nacional de Hogares (ENAHO)

ENAHO is a continuous, nationally representative household survey covering urban and rural areas across all 24 departments plus the Provincia Constitucional del Callao. It captures:

- Employment status and occupation (formal vs. informal)
- Income from independent work and micro-enterprises
- Business characteristics (sector, number of workers, registration status)
- Access to financial products, technology, and social protections
- Demographics of business owners (age, gender, education)

Because ENAHO surveys both formal and informal businesses, it provides the most complete picture of Peru's micro-enterprise universe — critically including the ~87% that operate informally and would be invisible in tax registry data alone.

### 2.2 Encuesta de Micro y Pequeña Empresa (EMYPE)

PRODUCE historically conducted the EMYPE to capture detailed operational characteristics of micro and small enterprises, including revenue structures, technology use, access to credit, and formalization barriers. While EMYPE has been conducted irregularly, its methodological framework informs the annual *MIPYME en Cifras* reports.

### 2.3 Demografía Empresarial (Business Demography)

Published quarterly by INEI using SUNAT's taxpayer registry, this bulletin tracks:

- Total stock of active enterprises (those with valid RUC registrations)
- New enterprise creation ("altas") and closures ("bajas")
- Geographic distribution by department
- Sector breakdown by CIIU Rev. 4 classification
- Legal organization form (persona natural, SAC, SRL, etc.)

**Important caveat:** This source only captures formally registered entities. It significantly undercounts total economic activity, as the majority of micro-enterprises operate without RUC registration.

## 3. Peru's MYPE Legal Framework

### 3.1 Law 28015 (2003) — Ley de Promoción y Formalización de la Micro y Pequeña Empresa

The foundational legislation establishing the legal definition and special regime for micro and small enterprises in Peru. Key provisions:

- **Microempresa:** Up to 10 workers, annual sales ≤150 UIT (Unidades Impositivas Tributarias)
- **Pequeña empresa:** Up to 100 workers, annual sales ≤1,700 UIT
- Created a special labor regime (*Régimen Laboral Especial*) with reduced obligations for employers
- Established simplified tax regimes and reduced municipal licensing requirements
- Mandated creation of promotional programs for formalization, training, and market access

### 3.2 Decreto Supremo 007-2008-TR — Texto Único Ordenado (TUO) de la Ley MYPE

This consolidated text updated and expanded the original framework:

- Refined definitions based solely on annual sales thresholds (removing worker count for classification purposes in some contexts)
- Current thresholds: Microempresa ≤150 UIT (~S/ 742,500 for 2024); Pequeña empresa 150–1,700 UIT
- Established the **REMYPE** (Registro Nacional de la Micro y Pequeña Empresa) as the official registry
- Defined benefits including access to state procurement (40% reserved for MYPEs), subsidized health insurance (SIS), and optional pension participation for micro-enterprise workers
- Created the Régimen MYPE Tributario at SUNAT for simplified tax compliance

### 3.3 Subsequent Reforms

- **Decreto Legislativo 1086 (2008):** Extended and made permanent the special labor regime
- **Ley 30056 (2013):** Removed the worker-count criterion entirely, defining enterprise size solely by annual sales
- **Decreto Legislativo 1269 (2016):** Created the Régimen MYPE Tributario (RMT) at SUNAT with preferential tax rates (10% on first 15 UIT of net income vs. standard 29.5%)

## 4. Key Statistics: The Scale of Peru's Micro-Enterprise Universe

### 4.1 Total Enterprise Stock (2024)

According to INEI's *Demografía Empresarial IV Trimestre 2024*:

- **3,537,639 active enterprises** were registered in the DCEE as of December 31, 2024 — a **6.9% increase** over the same period in 2023
- Of these, **93.9% are microenterprises** (≤150 UIT in annual sales), **5.2% are small enterprises**, and **0.2% are medium enterprises** (PRODUCE, *MIPYME en Cifras 2023*)
- This translates to approximately **2,168,708 formal microenterprises** (2023 data)

However, this drastically understates reality. Including informal enterprises (those without RUC):

- **Total MYPEs (formal + informal): approximately 6 million** as of 2024, according to ComexPerú analysis of ENAHO data
- MYPEs represent **99.7% of all enterprises** in Peru

### 4.2 Enterprise Dynamics (Q4 2024)

- **77,736 new enterprises** created or reactivated in Q4 2024 (+12.9% vs. Q4 2023)
- **292,878 enterprises closed** (bajas) — though this figure was inflated by SUNAT's inclusion of the Régimen MYPE Tributario in its automated closure process
- **Net variation: −215,142** in Q4 2024 (largely a statistical artifact of SUNAT's administrative cleanup)
- Birth rate: 2.20% | Mortality rate: 8.28% | Net rate: −6.08%

### 4.3 Geographic Distribution

Lima dominates: **35.4% of all new enterprises** in Q1 2024 were created in Lima Metropolitana. Key regional concentrations:

| Region | Share of MYPEs | Key Sectors |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Lima | 22.3% (by MYPE owners) / 45.5% (formal MIPYME) | Services, Commerce |
| Piura | 7.2% | Agriculture, Commerce |
| La Libertad | 6.9% | Agriculture, Commerce |
| Cajamarca | 6.8% | Agriculture |
| Cusco | 6.4% | Tourism, Commerce |
| Arequipa | ~6% | Services, Manufacturing |

### 4.4 Sector Breakdown

According to PRODUCE (2023 formal MIPYME data) and ENAHO (all MYPEs):

- **Agropecuario (Agriculture):** 39.3% of all MYPE owners
- **Servicios (Services):** 26.9%
- **Comercio (Commerce/Retail):** 21.8%
- **Manufactura (Manufacturing):** 8.0%
- **Construcción (Construction):** ~3%
- **Minería, Pesca (Mining, Fishing):** <2% combined

Formally registered MIPYMEs show a different distribution weighted toward commerce (~1 million units) and services, as agricultural micro-enterprises are disproportionately informal.

## 5. Informality: The Defining Feature

### 5.1 Business Informality

Peru's micro-enterprise informality rate is staggering and worsening:

| Year | MYPE Informality Rate | Micro Informality | Small Informality |
|------|----------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 2018 | 82.6% | 89.4% | 48.7% |
| 2019 | 83.3% | 89.8% | 49.5% |
| 2020 | 86.0% | 92.2% | 45.3% |
| 2021 | 86.1% | 91.9% | 49.6% |
| 2022 | 84.8% | 91.3% | 48.3% |
| 2023 | 86.3% | ~91.5% | ~48% |
| 2024 | **86.8%** | ~92% | ~48% |

*Sources: PRODUCE–OGEIEE, ComexPerú analysis of ENAHO*

**Key implication:** The total informal MYPE universe is approximately **5.2 million units** — enterprises operating without RUC, without formal accounting, and outside the tax and labor regulatory framework.

### 5.2 Employment Informality in MYPEs

- **81.3% of workers in MIPYMEs** are employed informally — no social security, no paid vacations, no gratifications (PRODUCE, 2023)
- **82.1% of MYPE workers are family members** of the business owner, and **95.9% of those are unpaid family workers**
- Only **1.5% of MYPE workers have social security** coverage
- Only **18.7% of MYPE employment is formal** (2023)

### 5.3 Regional Informality

Highest informality: Huancavelica (97.1%), Apurímac (96.9%), Puno (96.8%), Amazonas (96.3%), Ayacucho (96.1%)

Lowest (still very high): Lima (70.7%), Callao (68.1%)

Sectoral informality: Agriculture (99.0%), Mining (92.6%), Commerce (89.3%), Services (78.7%), Construction (76.2%), Manufacturing (73.8%)

## 6. Revenue Profiles of Micro-Enterprises

### 6.1 Aggregate Sales

- Total MYPE sales in **2024: S/ 154,380 million** — a decline of **0.8%** from S/ 155,697 million in 2023
- MYPEs contribute approximately **14.2% of GDP** (2024), down 1.4 pp from prior year
- MIPYMEs contribute approximately **30.9% of Valor Agregado Nacional** (PRODUCE, 2023)

### 6.2 Worker Income

- **Formal MYPE workers:** Average monthly income of **S/ 860** in 2024 (down 20.5% from S/ 1,082 in 2023)
- **Informal MYPE workers:** Average monthly income of **S/ 770** in 2024 (down 10.3%)
- Services sector MYPE workers receive the highest average remuneration: **S/ 728/month** (2023)
- Significant seasonality exists, particularly in agriculture-dependent and tourism-dependent regions

### 6.3 Financial Inclusion

- **54.1% of MYPEs** had at least one formal financial product in 2024 (up from 35.1% in 2019)
- **52.9% used formal savings methods** (up from 31.9% in 2019)
- Only **28.5% of formal MIPYMEs** accessed formal credit (2023)
- **75.3% of MYPEs keep no accounting records whatsoever** (2023)
- **37.1% of total regulated credit** went to MIPYMEs (2023)

## 7. Technology Adoption

### 7.1 National Connectivity (INEI, Q4 2024 / Q2 2025)

- **58.4% of households** have internet access (Q4 2024); rising to **60.1%** (Q2 2025)
- Lima Metropolitana: **79.4–80.5%** household internet access
- Rest urban: **59.7–61.6%**
- Rural: **21.7–23.6%**
- **80.1–80.2% of the population 6+** uses the internet
- **89.0–89.8% of internet users** access via cellphone
- **95.1–95.4% of households** have at least one member with a cellphone

### 7.2 Mobile and Digital (DataReportal, January 2024)

- **37.9 million cellular mobile connections** (109.8% of population — many have multiple SIMs)
- **25.78 million internet users** (74.7% penetration)
- **24.05 million social media users** (69.7% of population)
- Internet access via mobile: **89.8%** of all internet users
- Median mobile internet speed: **17.41 Mbps**

### 7.3 WhatsApp and Messaging (2024–2026)

WhatsApp is the dominant communication platform for Peruvian micro-entrepreneurs:

- **~26 million WhatsApp users** in Peru (76% of population)
- **~19.4 million weekly active users** on Android alone (SensorTower, Q3 2024)
- **WhatsApp Business: ~4.4 million active users** in Peru (SensorTower, Q4 2024)
- **~1.3 million businesses** estimated to use WhatsApp Business (AuroraInbox, 2026)
- **45% year-on-year growth** in WhatsApp Business adoption — the highest rate in Latin America alongside Colombia
- WhatsApp Business API adoption rate: **8–11%** among medium and large companies

### 7.4 Digital Tool Usage Among Micro-Entrepreneurs

- MYPE technology adoption remains low: most micro-enterprises use only a cellphone and WhatsApp
- Only **~18.5%** of micro-enterprises use any form of digital tool beyond basic mobile communication
- Formal financial products are increasingly accessed via mobile banking apps
- Point-of-sale digital payments (Yape, Plin) have seen explosive adoption — a critical complementary infrastructure

## 8. Demographics of Micro-Enterprise Owners

### 8.1 Gender

- **51% of MYPE employer-owners are women** (ComexPerú/ENAHO, 2023)
- **52.9% of new persona natural businesses** registered in Q1 2024 were led by women
- **39.3% of all entrepreneurs leading a MYPE** are women (PRODUCE, 2023 — broader definition including independent workers)
- Sector variation: **73.9% of commerce MYPEs** are led by women; **45.7% of production MYPEs**; only **36% of services MYPEs**

### 8.2 Age

- **Average age of MYPE owners: 43.9 years** (ComexPerú, 2023)
- PRODUCE reports average age of **46 years** (including independent workers)
- Average age of MYPE workers: **33 years**
- Range: 14 to 93 years among business owners

### 8.3 Education

- **73.4% of MYPE entrepreneurs** have primary or secondary education as their highest level
- **18.1%** have completed university-level education
- Average education level among MYPE owners: **secondary complete**
- Services sector owners have slightly higher education (equivalent to incomplete technical studies)

### 8.4 Household Context

- **52% of MYPE owners are heads of household** — business income is the primary or sole household income source
- **82.1% of employees are family members**, with **95.9% of family workers being unpaid**

## 9. Growth Trends and Enterprise Dynamics

### 9.1 Formation and Survival

- **MIPYME formal enterprises grew 2.2% in 2023**, reaching 2,294,284 (PRODUCE)
- Total MYPEs (formal + informal) grew **0.6% in 2024** to approximately 6 million
- In Q1 2024, **71,070 new enterprises** were created — 36.4% in Lima Metropolitana
- **61% of new enterprises** registered as persona natural businesses

### 9.2 Mortality and Survival Rates

- Enterprise mortality is high: PRODUCE tracks cohort survival rates showing significant attrition in the first 1–3 years
- The Q4 2024 "negative net variation" of −215,142 was primarily a data artifact from SUNAT cleaning RMT registrations, not actual business closures
- **Definitive closures decreased 20.2%** in Q4 2024 — suggesting underlying business survival is actually improving

### 9.3 Formalization Trends

The trend is negative:
- Formal MYPEs with RUC **decreased 3.1%** between 2023 and 2024
- Informal MYPEs **increased 1.2%** in the same period (~40,000 new informal businesses net)
- The **Índice de Capacidad Formal (ICF)** declined 0.8% in 2024
- Despite more MYPE promotional programs, bureaucratic barriers persist (e.g., licensing in Madre de Dios takes 12+ days)

## 10. Necessity Entrepreneurship

The "necessity entrepreneurship" phenomenon is a defining structural feature of Peru's micro-enterprise landscape:

- **52.4% of MYPE owners in 2024** started their business due to economic necessity (up from 48.8% in 2019)
- **53.4% in 2023** cited necessity as the primary motivation (ComexPerú)
- Other motivations: improving personal/family income (18.8–19.4%), desire for independence (16.5–17.9%)
- The steady increase in necessity-driven entrepreneurship correlates with economic contraction, rising unemployment, and declining formal job opportunities
- Necessity entrepreneurs are more likely to be informal, have lower revenues, and shorter business lifespans

**Implication:** The majority of Peru's micro-enterprises are not growth-oriented startups but survival mechanisms — a critical insight for platform design and pricing.

## 11. REMYPE: The Formalization Gateway

The **Registro Nacional de la Micro y Pequeña Empresa (REMYPE)**, administered by the Ministerio de Trabajo y Promoción del Empleo (MTPE), is the official registry granting access to MYPE benefits:

### 11.1 Requirements
- Valid RUC (registered with SUNAT)
- At least one employee (the owner alone is insufficient for persona natural or EIRL)
- Not operating in prohibited sectors (bars, discos, casinos, gambling)
- Online registration via MTPE portal using RUC and Clave SOL

### 11.2 Benefits of Registration
- Access to state procurement (40% reserved for MYPEs)
- Reduced labor costs under the Régimen Laboral Especial
- Subsidized health insurance (SIS — 50% state contribution for micro-enterprises)
- Optional pension participation for micro-enterprise workers
- Reduced fines and labor penalties
- Access to factoring/invoice financing programs (COFIDE)
- Training programs in business management, marketing, and productivity

### 11.3 Enrollment Reality

REMYPE enrollment remains low relative to the total MYPE universe. While exact current enrollment figures are not published prominently, the structural data tells the story:

- Only **~2.3 million formal MIPYMEs** exist (2023) out of an estimated **~6 million total MYPEs**
- Of formal MIPYMEs, an even smaller subset is registered in REMYPE (registration requires having at least one employee beyond the owner)
- The **requirement of at least one worker** effectively excludes the vast majority of one-person micro-enterprises, which constitute the bulk of the informal economy
- Many MYPEs that technically qualify don't register because they are unaware of the benefits or find the process burdensome

## 12. Implications for the Yaya Platform

### 12.1 Addressable Market Sizing

**Total Addressable Market (TAM):**
- ~6 million MYPEs in Peru (2024)
- ~26 million WhatsApp users; ~4.4 million WhatsApp Business active users
- Intersection: micro-entrepreneurs on WhatsApp ≈ **3–4 million** (assuming ~50–65% of MYPE owners are active WhatsApp users, given 76% national penetration and lower rural connectivity)

**Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM):**
- Urban MYPEs with smartphone access and basic digital literacy: **~2.5–3 million**
- Of these, those in commerce and services (most receptive to digital tools): **~1.5–2 million**

**Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM) — Year 1–3:**
- Targeting Lima + top 5 urban centers, commerce/services, with active WhatsApp usage: **100,000–300,000** realistic initial addressable segment

### 12.2 Pricing Sensitivity

Critical constraints:
- Average formal MYPE worker income: **S/ 860/month**; informal: **S/ 770/month**
- **75.3% keep no records** — suggesting near-zero current spend on business tools
- **52.4% started out of necessity** — extreme price sensitivity
- Total MYPE sales declined 0.8% in 2024 — businesses are under pressure

**Pricing implications:**
- Free tier is **essential** — most users won't pay upfront
- Paid tier must be **<S/ 30/month** (~$8 USD) to be feasible for formal micro-enterprises
- **<S/ 15/month** (~$4 USD) for informal/necessity entrepreneurs
- Transaction-based pricing (per message, per transaction) may be more palatable than subscriptions
- Value must be immediately tangible: save time, increase sales, reduce errors

### 12.3 Feature Priorities

Based on the data, highest-impact features for this market:

1. **Simple accounting/record-keeping** — 75.3% keep no records; even basic income/expense tracking via WhatsApp messages would be transformative
2. **Invoice and receipt generation** — supporting formalization; integrating with SUNAT's electronic invoicing system
3. **Inventory management** — commerce MYPEs (21.8% of all) need stock tracking
4. **Customer communication automation** — leveraging WhatsApp's native Business features
5. **Financial product access** — 54.1% already have formal financial products; bridging to credit applications
6. **Supplier ordering** — streamlining procurement for commerce businesses
7. **Basic analytics** — daily/weekly sales summaries, popular products
8. **Tax compliance tools** — RMT regime simplification, IGV calculations
9. **Voice-first interface** — given education levels (73.4% secondary or below), voice notes and audio responses reduce literacy barriers
10. **Bilingual support** — Quechua/Spanish in highland regions

### 12.4 Market Entry Strategy

- **Lead with commerce-sector women entrepreneurs** — 73.9% of commerce MYPEs are women-led; they are the most active WhatsApp users and most receptive to organizational tools
- **Lima first, then Arequipa/Trujillo/Piura** — highest density of digitally connected MYPEs
- **Partner with Yape/Plin ecosystem** — digital payment adoption is the wedge for digital tool adoption
- **REMYPE integration** — offer formalization assistance as value-add (help businesses register, access benefits)

## 13. Regional Comparison: Mexico (INEGI) and Colombia (DANE)

### 13.1 Mexico — INEGI Censos Económicos 2024

| Metric | Peru | Mexico | Comparison |
|--------|------|--------|------------|
| Total economic units | ~6M MYPEs | 5.47M (private sector) | Similar scale relative to population |
| Informal units | 86.8% of MYPEs | 64.3% of all units | Peru significantly more informal |
| Informal GDP contribution | ~14.2% (MYPE sales/GDP) | 25.4% of GDP | Mexico's informal sector more productive per unit |
| Labor informality | 81.3% in MIPYMEs | 54.4% national rate | Peru much worse |
| Women in informal sector | 51% of MYPE owners are women | 54% of informal unit workers are women | Similar gender dynamics |

**Key differences:** Mexico's Censos Económicos (comprehensive economic census every 5 years, most recently reference year 2023) provides granular unit-level data Peru lacks. Mexico's informal economy, while massive (3.5 million informal units), contributes significantly more value per unit (25.4% of GDP from informal sector) compared to Peru's fragmented micro-enterprise base. Mexico's higher formalization rate (35.7%) reflects stronger institutional capacity and the IMSS/SAT enforcement apparatus.

### 13.2 Colombia — DANE EMICRON / IMIE 2024

| Metric | Peru | Colombia | Comparison |
|--------|------|----------|------------|
| Enterprise informality (multidimensional) | 86.8% (MYPEs) | 90.2% (micronegocios) | Colombia slightly higher by IMIE methodology |
| Labor informality | 81.3% in MIPYMEs | 55% national; 84.6% in microenterprises | Comparable at micro-enterprise level |
| Lowest-income informality | ~92% for micro | 98.2% (<COP 1M/month) | Both near-total for smallest |
| Accounting records | 75.3% keep none | 95.3% informal on accounting | Colombia's measurement is stricter |
| Digital infrastructure | 76% WhatsApp penetration | 73% WhatsApp penetration | Peru slightly ahead |

**Key insight:** Colombia's DANE provides a more sophisticated multidimensional informality index (IMIE, based on Alkire-Foster methodology), measuring informality across four dimensions per CONPES 3956. Peru could benefit from a similar approach. Colombia's 90.2% multidimensional informality rate among micronegocios is remarkably close to Peru's figures, suggesting structural similarities: both countries have massive informal micro-enterprise sectors driven by necessity entrepreneurship, low productivity, family labor, and minimal compliance with regulatory frameworks.

**WhatsApp Business adoption** is growing fastest in Peru and Colombia (45% and 42% year-on-year respectively), suggesting both markets are at an inflection point for WhatsApp-based business tools.

### 13.3 Cross-Country Opportunity

The Yaya platform's addressable market across Peru, Mexico, and Colombia combined represents:

- **~15+ million micro-enterprises** with high informality rates
- **~12–15 million WhatsApp Business potential users** across the three countries
- Shared characteristics: Spanish-speaking, necessity entrepreneurship, low digital tool adoption, high WhatsApp usage, growing mobile payment ecosystems
- A platform proven in one market has natural expansion pathways to the others

## 14. Data Gaps and Limitations

### 14.1 INEI Data Limitations

1. **Formal vs. total universe:** INEI's DCEE/Business Demography only captures SUNAT-registered entities. The ~5.2 million informal MYPEs are estimated via ENAHO sampling, introducing uncertainty.

2. **ENAHO sampling limitations:** While nationally representative, ENAHO's sample size (~40,000 households) limits the granularity of sub-regional or sector-specific estimates for micro-enterprises, particularly in less-populated departments.

3. **Revenue data scarcity:** Precise revenue distributions for micro-enterprises are extremely difficult to capture. Informal businesses have no obligation to report income, and ENAHO self-reported income data is subject to significant underreporting bias.

4. **Technology usage gaps:** INEI's ICT module measures household-level connectivity and individual internet usage, but does not specifically survey *business-purpose* technology adoption among micro-entrepreneurs. There is no official data on how many micro-enterprises use WhatsApp for business, digital payment tools for transactions, or any business management software.

5. **Temporal lag:** The most comprehensive MIPYME data (PRODUCE report) references 2023. ENAHO microdata for 2024 becomes available with delay. Real-time market conditions may differ.

6. **Survival rate methodology:** Enterprise mortality data is confounded by SUNAT's administrative actions (mass deactivations of dormant RUCs), making it difficult to distinguish actual business closures from registry cleanup.

7. **Informality measurement inconsistency:** Different sources define informality differently — ComexPerú uses RUC registration, PRODUCE uses employment-based measures, and multidimensional approaches (as Colombia's IMIE) are not yet applied in Peru. This creates confusion in cross-source comparisons.

### 14.2 Critical Missing Data for Platform Development

- **Digital payment adoption by micro-enterprises:** No official data on Yape/Plin usage among MYPEs
- **WhatsApp usage patterns:** No survey data on how micro-entrepreneurs use WhatsApp for business (customer communication, orders, payments)
- **Willingness-to-pay for digital tools:** No published research on Peruvian micro-entrepreneur price sensitivity for SaaS/business tools
- **Literacy and digital literacy levels** among MYPE owners, beyond general education statistics
- **Seasonal revenue patterns** by sector and region for micro-enterprises
- **Customer acquisition channels** used by micro-enterprises (word of mouth vs. social media vs. marketplaces)

### 14.3 Recommendations for Primary Research

To fill these gaps before platform launch, Yaya should consider:

1. **Field survey of 500–1,000 micro-entrepreneurs** in Lima, Arequipa, and Trujillo focusing on digital tool usage, WhatsApp behavior, payment methods, and willingness-to-pay
2. **WhatsApp usage audit:** Analysis of WhatsApp Business adoption patterns via SensorTower/App Annie data
3. **Partnership with PRODUCE or ComexPerú** to access ENAHO microdata for custom cross-tabulations
4. **Pilot testing** with 100 micro-entrepreneurs to validate feature prioritization and pricing
