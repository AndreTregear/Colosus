# Vertical Opportunity: Construction Sector in Peru
## WhatsApp-Based AI Platform for Construction SMBs

**Date:** 2026-03-21
**Author:** Yaya Research
**Status:** Initial Research

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Peru's construction sector represents one of the most compelling vertical opportunities for a WhatsApp-based AI business platform targeting SMBs. The sector contributes approximately 5.6% of national GDP, is growing at 3.5–4.6% annually, and is dominated by micro and small enterprises operating in a 60–75% informal environment. These businesses already use WhatsApp as their primary digital tool but lack any structured business management software. The combination of massive market size (~100,000+ micro-construction enterprises), high WhatsApp penetration, acute operational pain points, and zero competition from specialized contech platforms creates an ideal beachhead vertical.

## 2. Sector Size and Economic Contribution

### 2.1 GDP Contribution

The construction sector consistently accounts for **5.6% of Peru's national production index** (Producción Nacional), according to INEI's Dirección Nacional de Indicadores Económicos. In absolute terms, GDP from construction reached approximately **PEN 8,853 million** (constant 2007 prices) in Q2 2025, with projections trending toward PEN 11,727 million by 2026 (Trading Economics / INEI data).

Peru's total GDP stood at approximately **USD 289 billion** in 2024, placing the construction sector's direct contribution at roughly **USD 16–17 billion** annually. When including indirect effects (materials supply chain, transport, equipment rental, financial services), the construction ecosystem's total economic footprint is estimated at 2–3x the direct GDP figure.

### 2.2 Growth Trajectory

| Year | Construction GDP Growth | Key Driver |
|------|------------------------|------------|
| 2023 | -8.7% | Political crisis, social unrest, El Niño |
| 2024 | +2.2% (CAPECO) / +4.6% (ResearchAndMarkets) | Public investment recovery (+10.5%) |
| 2025 (projected) | +3.5% (CAPECO/BCRP) / +3.8% (ResearchAndMarkets) | Mining investment, infrastructure stimulus |
| 2026–2029 | +3.6% avg. annual | Educational facilities, roads, renewables |

The BCRP projects public works growth of **4.5%** for 2025, with cement consumption expected to increase by **3.2%**, signaling recovery in private investment, particularly in housing.

INEI reported that construction grew **5.3% in Q1 2025** year-over-year, with gross fixed investment surging **9.7%**, indicating strong momentum entering the current period. Monthly data for early 2025 shows consistent positive readings: January (+4.13%), February (+5.91%), March (+5.22%), May (+6.04%).

### 2.3 2025–2026 Outlook

Growth will be supported by:
- **Capital expenditure increases:** +7.3% for regional governments, +24.2% for local governments (2025 budget vs. 2024)
- **Mining investment:** 84 mining projects across 17 regions with PEN 3.8 billion (~USD 1 billion) in planned investment
- **Housing programs:** Historic budget of **S/2,400 million** for housing programs in 2025
- **Infrastructure:** Line 2 of Lima Metro, airport expansions, road infrastructure

## 3. Industry Structure

### 3.1 The Three Tiers

Peru's construction industry operates in three distinct tiers with dramatically different levels of formality, technology adoption, and business sophistication:

**Tier 1: Large Formal Companies (~200–400 firms)**
- Major developers and contractors (Graña y Montero/Aenza, COSAPI, JJC Contratistas, Odebrecht legacy operations)
- Handle mega-projects: mining infrastructure, Line 2 Metro, hospitals, large commercial developments
- Use BIM, ERP systems, formal project management
- BIM adoption grew from 25% to 39% at project level between 2017–2020 among Lima contractors (ASCE study)
- Account for ~25–30% of sector output

**Tier 2: Small and Medium Formal Enterprises (~600–2,000 firms)**
- Registered construction companies, "Entidades Técnicas" for government housing programs
- CAPECO identified approximately **600 Entidades Técnicas** operating in the Techo Propio program as of 2017, with ~180 organized in associations
- These firms handle mid-size projects: social housing developments, small commercial buildings, renovations
- Manage 5–50 workers per project
- Account for ~10–15% of sector output

**Tier 3: Informal Micro-enterprises and Independent Workers (~100,000+ operators)**
- **Maestros de obra** (master builders): Experienced workers who manage small construction projects independently
- **Albañiles** (masons/laborers): Skilled and semi-skilled workers, often organized in cuadrillas (crews)
- **Autoconstrucción operators**: Individuals and families who self-build with hired informal labor
- Account for **60–75% of all construction activity** in Peru
- Produce approximately **100,000 informal housing units per year** (CAPECO/Constructivo, 2021)
- **3 out of every 4 homes** built in Peru are constructed informally (Andina/MVCS)

### 3.2 Employment

According to CAPECO's IEC reports, approximately **384,500 workers** were employed in construction in Lima Metropolitana alone (March–May 2024 trimester), though this was 12.1% below the historic peak reached in 2022.

Nationwide, construction is one of the most important sectors for informal male employment, comprising approximately **20% of men's informal employment** in Metropolitan Lima and urban Peru (WIEGO, 2023). Total informal employment in Peru stands at approximately **77% nationally** (2021), with construction being one of the most heavily informal sectors.

### 3.3 The Autoconstrucción Economy

Self-construction (autoconstrucción) is the dominant mode of building in Peru:
- Represents **60–75% of all construction expenditure** in the country (Construction21/CAPECO data)
- Drives the majority of cement consumption—CAPECO's supplier surveys show informal construction accounts for **33–39% of materials supplier revenue**
- Operates through networks of maestros de obra who quote jobs via word-of-mouth, manage small crews, and purchase materials from local ferreterías
- Quality and safety are major concerns: an estimated megaseism in Lima could destroy 350,000 homes and render 625,000 uninhabitable, largely due to informal construction practices (CENEPRED estimates cited by CAPECO)

## 4. Typical Business Operations of Construction SMBs

### 4.1 Quoting and Estimation

- **Current process:** Maestros de obra visit a site, mentally estimate materials and labor, and provide a verbal or handwritten quote
- Quotes are typically communicated via WhatsApp voice messages or brief text messages
- No standardized formats; wide variation in pricing accuracy
- Clients often receive 2–3 quotes from different maestros, with no easy way to compare
- Material prices are checked by calling or visiting 2–3 ferreterías

### 4.2 Materials Procurement

- Small operators purchase materials project-by-project from local **ferreterías** (hardware/building supply stores)
- No bulk purchasing power; no negotiated pricing
- Cash or short-term credit ("fiado") relationships with trusted ferretería owners
- Price comparison is manual and time-consuming
- Materials waste is significant (estimated 15–25% overbuying due to poor estimation)

### 4.3 Labor Management

- Workers are recruited through personal networks and WhatsApp groups
- No formal employment contracts for informal segment
- Daily pay (jornal) ranging from S/50–120 depending on skill level and region
- No scheduling tools; coordination via phone calls and WhatsApp messages
- High worker turnover; crews change frequently between projects
- Worker mafias (mafias de colocación) in some areas control placement and charge fees

### 4.4 Invoicing and Payment

- Informal sector operates almost entirely in cash
- No invoicing (no boletas or facturas)
- Payment milestones are verbal agreements (e.g., "50% at start, 50% at completion")
- Disputes over payment are common due to lack of documentation
- Formal SMBs use basic Excel or manual ledgers

### 4.5 Project Tracking

- No project management tools
- Progress communicated via WhatsApp photos and voice messages
- No cost tracking against budgets during execution
- Post-project accounting is minimal or non-existent

## 5. Technology Adoption

### 5.1 Current Digital Tool Usage

According to the DAI/Ipsos 2021 survey of 997 MSMEs in Peru:
- **78%** of MSMEs used digital tools for business purposes during COVID-19
- **78%** primarily used **mobile phones** to connect to the internet (not laptops/PCs)
- **55%** of online MSMEs used **WhatsApp** to communicate with customers
- **48%** used WhatsApp to communicate with suppliers
- **42%** used WhatsApp for marketing
- **69%** specifically cited WhatsApp as helping them adapt to COVID-19

For construction specifically:
- **81%** of MSMEs in manufacturing and industry (which includes construction) reported digital tool use during COVID-19—higher than other sectors
- WhatsApp is the **de facto business operating system** for construction SMBs
- Excel is used by a minority of more formalized operations
- Paper notebooks (cuadernos) remain common for tracking expenses and measurements
- No construction-specific software adoption at the SMB/informal level

### 5.2 The WhatsApp-First Reality

WhatsApp in Peru is not just a messaging app—it is the primary business communication platform:
- **WhatsApp is the most popular free messaging app in Peru** (Hootsuite annual report)
- 99% of Venezuelan migrants (a growing segment of construction labor) prefer WhatsApp for information (IFRC study)
- Construction workers coordinate via WhatsApp groups for: job availability, crew assembly, site directions, material requests
- Clients communicate with contractors primarily through WhatsApp
- Photo/video documentation of work already happens organically via WhatsApp

This means a WhatsApp-native AI platform would meet construction SMBs exactly where they already operate, with zero friction for adoption.

## 6. Pain Points

### 6.1 Cash Flow Management
- Projects are typically funded in stages by the client, but delays are common
- Materials must be purchased upfront; labor is paid weekly
- Cash flow gaps are the #1 cause of project delays and business failure
- No financial tools or lending products designed for informal construction operators

### 6.2 Materials Price Volatility
- Cement, steel (fierro), and aggregate prices fluctuate significantly
- No visibility into pricing trends or ability to lock in prices
- UNACEM and Pacasmayo (the two cement oligopolists) set prices that ripple through the chain
- International steel prices impact local rebar (fierro corrugado) costs
- COVID-19 and supply chain disruptions caused 20–40% price spikes in key materials (2020–2022)

### 6.3 Labor Scheduling and Reliability
- Workers often take on multiple jobs simultaneously
- No-shows are common, causing project delays
- No way to track worker availability across projects
- Skill verification is entirely word-of-mouth

### 6.4 Payment Collection
- Clients delay or default on payments, especially after work completion
- No documentation to support claims or disputes
- No digital payment infrastructure in informal segment
- Yape and Plin adoption growing but not yet standard in construction transactions

### 6.5 Regulatory Compliance
- Building permits (licencias de construcción) are complex and vary by municipality
- Environmental and safety requirements are poorly understood
- Tax obligations for formal SMBs add compliance burden
- CAPECO's "Construyendo Formalidad" initiative highlights 5 key reform areas but progress is slow

### 6.6 Estimation Accuracy
- Material waste from poor estimation costs 15–25% of project budgets
- No standardized reference for material quantities per square meter by project type
- Rework due to errors adds 10–20% to project timelines

## 7. Government Programs and Public Investment

### 7.1 Techo Propio

The flagship social housing program provides a **Bono Familiar Habitacional (BFH):**
- **To buy:** up to S/58,300 (10.6 UIT) for prioritized social housing
- **To build:** S/33,000 on one's own lot
- **To improve:** S/12,650
- Eligibility: families earning less than S/3,715/month (buying) or S/2,706/month (building)
- **First 2025 convocation:** 26,604 bonos distributed across all 25 regions
- **Exceptional bonos** for 7 remote regions with 15–20% premium on BFH value
- Between 2008–2017, SME construction firms ("Entidades Técnicas") built **250,000 housing units** under Techo Propio (CAPECO/Andina)
- Jan–Aug 2025: ~39,000 BFH disbursed, a **73% increase** vs. same period 2024

### 7.2 Crédito MiVivienda

- Mortgage credit program with government subsidy (Bono del Buen Pagador)
- **67% of 2025 disbursements** went to "Bono Verde" certified green housing
- Expanding to include second-use housing, rental, and accessibility options

### 7.3 Reconstrucción con Cambios (RCC)

Created after the devastating 2017 coastal El Niño, this program manages reconstruction of:
- Schools, hospitals, roads, bridges, and flood protection infrastructure
- Primarily in Piura, La Libertad, Lambayeque, and Áncash regions
- Uses Government-to-Government (G2G) agreements with UK, France, Australia, and South Korea
- Multi-billion dollar program extending through 2025+

### 7.4 Overall Public Investment

- **S/2,400 million** historic budget for housing programs in 2025
- S/3,600+ million invested in 110,000+ housing bonos during current administration
- 593 water and sanitation works completed (2022–2025) at S/5,882 million
- 205 urban infrastructure projects at S/558.5 million

### 7.5 Opportunity for Yaya Platform

Every Techo Propio and RCC project requires an Entidad Técnica (small construction firm) to manage execution. These ~600 firms are ideal early adopters:
- Formalized enough to need documentation and reporting
- Small enough (5–20 person teams) to lack enterprise software
- Must report progress to Fondo MIVIVIENDA
- Already have WhatsApp as primary communication tool
- Government program participation could be a qualification incentive for platform adoption

## 8. Materials Supply Chain

### 8.1 Cement Market (Oligopoly)

Peru's cement market is controlled by two dominant players:
- **UNACEM (Unión Andina de Cementos):** ~52% market share, controls Lima and central regions, brands include Andino and Sol
- **Cementos Pacasmayo (Hochschild Group):** ~22% market share, dominant in northern Peru
- **Yura (Gloria Group):** ~20% market share, dominant in southern Peru
- **Cementos Selva:** Small player in jungle regions

Cement is the single most important construction input in Peru, and its oligopolistic structure creates predictable pricing dynamics but also vulnerability to price manipulation.

### 8.2 Modern Retail

- **Sodimac (Falabella Group):** Largest home improvement retailer in Peru, ~40 stores
- **Promart (Intercorp Group):** Growing competitor, ~30 stores, strong digital presence
- **Maestro (also Falabella):** Mid-market positioning
- These retailers serve primarily end consumers and formal contractors
- Pricing is typically 10–20% above ferretería pricing for comparable products
- Growing e-commerce presence but still small percentage of revenue

### 8.3 Traditional Channel: Ferreterías

- Estimated **50,000–80,000 ferreterías** nationwide (no precise census exists)
- Range from micro-shops (one person, minimal inventory) to mid-size building supply warehouses
- Handle the vast majority of informal construction materials sales
- Operate on thin margins with personal relationships driving credit
- CAPECO surveys show ferreterías report **33–39% of their revenue** comes from informal construction
- Price negotiation is personal and opaque

### 8.4 Supply Chain Opportunity

A WhatsApp-based platform could serve as an intermediary layer:
- **Price transparency:** Aggregate ferretería pricing data for construction operators
- **Bulk ordering:** Combine orders from multiple small operators for better pricing
- **Delivery coordination:** Connect with emerging delivery platforms
- **Credit facilitation:** Use project and purchasing data to build credit profiles for operators

## 9. How a WhatsApp AI Platform Could Serve Construction

### 9.1 Core Features for Construction Vertical

**Quote Generation (Presupuestos)**
- User sends project description via WhatsApp: "Necesito presupuesto para construir segundo piso, 80m², material noble"
- AI generates itemized quote with material quantities, labor estimates, and total cost
- Based on local market prices (updatable) and standard construction ratios
- Output as formatted message or PDF attachment
- Can adjust for project type: vivienda, local comercial, cerco perimétrico, etc.

**Materials Ordering**
- AI suggests materials list based on quote
- Integration with ferretería partners for pricing and availability
- One-tap ordering with delivery coordination
- Price comparison across 3–5 local suppliers
- Materials tracking: ordered → delivered → consumed

**Worker Scheduling**
- Maintain a database of available workers by skill and location
- Request workers: "Necesito 2 albañiles y 1 fierrero para lunes en San Juan de Lurigancho"
- Workers confirm availability via WhatsApp
- Track worker assignments across projects
- Build reputation/reliability scores over time

**Project Cost Tracking**
- Log expenses via WhatsApp: photo of receipt + "compré 20 bolsas de cemento S/520"
- AI categorizes and tracks against budget
- Weekly cost summary: what was budgeted vs. what was spent
- Alert when overspending risk detected

**Invoice Generation**
- Generate professional invoices/boletas from project data
- Send to clients via WhatsApp
- Track payment status
- Support both formal (with RUC) and simplified formats

**Regulatory Assistance**
- Guide users through building permit requirements by municipality
- Document templates for common permits
- Calendar reminders for regulatory deadlines
- Basic structural calculation validation for autoconstrucción

### 9.2 User Personas

| Persona | Description | Key Needs | Monthly Volume |
|---------|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| Maestro de Obra | Independent master builder, manages 2–4 projects/month | Quotes, materials ordering, worker scheduling | High |
| Small Contractor | Formal SME, 5–20 employees, registered | Invoicing, cost tracking, compliance | Medium |
| Entidad Técnica | Social housing builder under Techo Propio | Government reporting, batch project management | Medium |
| Ferretería Owner | Building supply shop | Inventory, pricing, customer orders | High |
| Homeowner (Self-builder) | Individual managing own construction | Quote validation, materials pricing, contractor finder | Low per user, high volume |

## 11. Competitive Landscape

### 11.1 Contech in LATAM

**Construex (Ecuador/Mexico/LATAM)**
- B2B marketplace for construction materials supply chain
- Founded 2019, expanded to Mexico, Guatemala, Ecuador, Chile, Argentina
- Focus on large-to-mid-size contractors and suppliers
- 80% of revenue from Mexico; 1 million sellers on platform
- Not focused on micro/informal segment; not WhatsApp-native
- Raised venture capital from US investors

**Cemex Ventures / Top 50 Contech**
- Cemex's venture arm tracks global contech innovation
- Most startups focus on US/European markets
- LATAM identified as "gaining traction" but far behind US/Europe
- Few if any startups targeting informal construction micro-enterprises

### 11.2 Peru-Specific Construction Tech

**Virtually non-existent** at the SMB/informal level:
- No WhatsApp-based construction management tools identified
- No local contech startups targeting maestros de obra or small contractors
- BIM adoption is growing but only among large firms (25% → 39% project-level adoption in Lima, 2017–2020)
- Government BIM policy announced in 2019 but targets public construction, not SMBs

### 11.3 Adjacent Competitors

- **Yape/Plin:** Digital payment apps growing rapidly, could become payment layer
- **Promart/Sodimac e-commerce:** Online ordering of materials, but not integrated with project management
- **Generic accounting apps (Contífico, Alegra):** Not construction-specific, not WhatsApp-native
- **Excel/Google Sheets:** The de facto "competitor" for any formalized tracking

### 11.4 Competitive Moat

The key competitive advantages of a WhatsApp-native construction AI platform would be:
1. **Zero-install adoption:** No app to download, no account to create beyond WhatsApp
2. **Spanish-language, Peru-specific:** Material names, pricing, local regulations
3. **Network effects:** As more maestros and ferreterías join, value compounds
4. **Data moat:** Construction pricing, worker reliability, and project data build over time
5. **Informal-sector design:** Built for operators who don't have email, websites, or bank accounts

## 12. Case Studies: Construction Tech in Emerging Markets

### 12.1 Construex — LATAM Materials Marketplace

- **Model:** B2B marketplace connecting construction suppliers to contractors
- **Traction:** 1M+ sellers, primarily Mexico, expanding across LATAM
- **Key insight:** "80% of the market is offline" — construction supply chains are completely undigitized in LATAM
- **Revenue:** Transaction-based, with SaaS tools provided free to drive adoption
- **Lesson for Yaya:** The market is proven; the gap is at the bottom of the pyramid (micro-enterprises)

### 12.2 Ghar Banao / Infra.Market — India

- India's construction tech ecosystem has exploded, with Infra.Market reaching unicorn status
- Serves the fragmented contractor market with materials procurement, pricing, and logistics
- India parallels: massive informal construction sector, mobile-first population, rapid urbanization
- **Lesson for Yaya:** WhatsApp is already the business communication layer in India's construction SMB segment; AI overlay is the logical next step

### 12.3 M-Pesa Model — Kenya (Adjacent)

- Mobile money transformed financial services for informal economies across Africa
- Construction workers adopted M-Pesa for payment before any construction-specific tool existed
- **Lesson for Yaya:** Meet informal workers on the platform they already use; financial services follow

### 12.4 Built Environment in Africa

- Several African startups (Kobo360, Buildher) target construction logistics and workforce
- Mobile-first design is mandatory; SMS/WhatsApp integration drives adoption
- **Lesson for Yaya:** In markets with low digital literacy, voice messages and images are more effective than text-based interfaces

## 13. Risks

### 13.1 Seasonality
- Construction activity drops during rainy season (December–March in Sierra and Selva)
- Coastal regions (Lima, Piura, Trujillo) are less affected but still see reduced activity
- Government spending tends to spike in Q4, creating year-end surges
- **Mitigation:** Diversify across regions and project types; offer maintenance/renovation tracking during off-season

### 13.2 Informality Barriers
- 60–75% of construction operates informally with cash payments
- Informal operators may resist any tool that creates a digital paper trail (tax risk perception)
- Trust is personal and relationship-based; technology must not feel like surveillance
- **Mitigation:** Position as a tool for making more money, not for compliance; free tier with no registration requirements; privacy guarantees

### 13.3 Low Digital Literacy Among Workers
- Many albañiles have limited education (average informal worker has less than high school completion)
- Smartphone usage is widespread but proficiency varies
- Complex features may confuse rather than help
- **Mitigation:** Voice-message-first interface; visual/photo-based inputs; AI that understands colloquial construction Spanish; gradual feature introduction

### 13.4 Price Sensitivity
- Informal operators earn S/50–120/day; any subscription feels expensive
- Willingness to pay for software is near-zero in this segment historically
- **Mitigation:** Freemium model with clear ROI demonstration; transaction-based revenue that aligns with user's own revenue events

### 13.5 Macroeconomic and Political Risk
- Peru has experienced significant political instability (6 presidents in 5 years, 2018–2023)
- El Niño events can devastate construction activity in affected regions while creating reconstruction demand
- Currency volatility (Sol vs USD) affects imported material prices
- **Mitigation:** Lean operations; avoid over-investment before product-market fit; focus on recession-resistant segment (housing maintenance and repair)

### 13.6 Construction Mafia and Union Dynamics
- FTCCP (Federación de Trabajadores en Construcción Civil del Perú) and CAPECO negotiate sector-wide labor agreements
- Worker placement mafias operate in some regions, charging fees for job access
- Disrupting labor markets could attract unwanted attention
- **Mitigation:** Position as facilitator, not disruptor; work with existing networks rather than against them
